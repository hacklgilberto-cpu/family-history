Part Two: Into the Forest
The Black Forest Migration and the Craftsmen
1450–1700
When Blasius and Adam von Braun left Ottobeuren sometime in the middle years of the fifteenth century, they turned their faces westward toward the mountains that the Romans had called Silva Nigra, the Black Forest. It was a landscape utterly unlike the gentle Swabian farmland of their birth, a country of steep valleys and dark ridges where spruce and fir grew so thick that the forest floor remained in perpetual twilight even at midday. Here the streams ran swift and cold over granite boulders. Here the winters brought snow that might lie six feet deep from November to April. Here the old gods of the Germanic tribes had lingered longer than in the civilized lowlands, and the forests still harbored wolves and bears and wild boar. But here too were opportunities that Ottobeuren could no longer offer, for the Black Forest in the mid-fifteenth century was a frontier, a place where men with skill and ambition could carve out new lives far from the restrictive hierarchies of the monastic lands.

The Swabian Alb — The gentle rolling landscape the Braun family left behind when they turned westward toward the mountains

The Geography of Opportunity

The Black Forest was not a single entity but a series of parallel mountain ridges running roughly north to south, separated by deep valleys where the tributaries of the Rhine and the Danube found their sources. The northern end of the range lay not far west of Ottobeuren, rising abruptly from the Swabian plain. Towns like Calw and Pforzheim had existed since Roman times, and by the fifteenth century they were prosperous centers of craft and trade.

Calw in particular had become known for its woodworking guilds. The town sat on the river Nagold, which provided waterpower for mills and transport for finished goods. The surrounding forests offered timber in seemingly inexhaustible quantities. The craft guilds were old and well organized, but they were also pragmatic. The population losses of the plague years meant that skilled craftsmen could find work, and families with some resources could establish themselves if they were willing to learn the trade.

South of Calw, the forest grew higher and wilder. The central Black Forest reached elevations of over three thousand feet. Here the valleys narrowed to gorges, and the settlements clung to whatever level ground could be found along the streams. This was mining country, where men dug for silver and lead and iron in the mountain flanks, and where the need for timber created constant demand for woodcutters and charcoal burners.

Deeper still into the mountains lay the monastery lands. Sankt Märgen and Sankt Peter, two foundations that mirrored in the Black Forest what Ottobeuren had been in Swabia, stood on adjacent ridges in the high country south of Freiburg. Both monasteries held extensive lands, both needed administrators and craftsmen, both offered the kind of structured, hierarchical world that a family like the Brauns would have understood from their Ottobeuren origins.

The Black Forest in the fifteenth century was undergoing rapid development. Population was recovering from the plague, and new technologies were driving expansion. Water-powered mills became more efficient, allowing for mechanized sawing of timber and crushing of ore. The demand for wood products grew with every passing decade. By 1500, the forests around Calw and Pforzheim were being systematically harvested, with regulations to ensure sustainable cutting. The high Black Forest, more remote and difficult of access, remained largely untouched except around the monasteries and mining camps. It was a landscape that rewarded those willing to work hard in harsh conditions, offering in exchange a degree of independence that was increasingly rare in the more settled parts of Germany.

Calw on the Nagold — The woodworking center where the Braun family likely first settled in the Black Forest

The Calw Connection

The trail grows cold in the records between Ottobeuren and the Black Forest. We know that Blasius von Braun lived until 1510 and his brother Adam Luppert von Braun until 1499. We know that one or both of them had sons who continued the family line. We know that by the early sixteenth century, members of the Braun family were established in the Calw region. But the exact path they took, the exact year they left, the reasons they chose the Black Forest over other destinations — these are lost to time.

Calw in the mid to late 1450s was a town of perhaps two thousand souls, clustered along both banks of the Nagold. The town had walls and gates, a market square, several churches, a ducal castle on the heights above. The guilds controlled most economic activity. To work as a master craftsman required years of apprenticeship, journeyman wandering, and finally the production of a masterpiece to prove one's skill. But the guilds also recognized nobility, and a family bearing the "von" designation would have had certain advantages in negotiating entry into the craft economy.

Calmbach, a smaller settlement a few kilometers upstream, appears in the family records as a place of residence for Adolph von Braun and his wife Catharina Lauxen, both born around 1485. Whether this Adolph was a grandson of the brothers who left Ottobeuren, or perhaps a nephew, we cannot say with certainty. But the "von" remains in his name, and the Lauxen connection indicates local ties being formed through marriage.

Calmbach was primarily a woodworking center, known for the production of roof shingles and barrel components. The steep hillsides made farming difficult but provided easy access to standing timber. Men would fell trees on the slopes above the town, let the logs slide downhill to the river, and float them downstream to the sawmills. It was dangerous work. Every year men were crushed by rolling logs or drowned in the swift current trying to guide timber rafts through rapids. But it was profitable work for those who survived, and it required skills that could be learned through practice rather than formal apprenticeship.

The Black Forest — Dense spruce and fir forests where winter brought snow six feet deep and summers were brief

Into the High Forest

The next clear point in the family geography comes from a single name and place: Hans Peter "Mang" von Braun, born 1505 in Haiterbach, died 1551 in Sankt Märgen. This man represents the family's deepest penetration into the Black Forest interior, and his death in a monastery town suggests connections that would prove crucial for how the Braun line eventually merged with other families.

Haiterbach lies northwest of Calw, still in the lower reaches of the forest. But Sankt Märgen sits at nearly nine hundred meters elevation on a ridge in the high Black Forest, accessible only by steep and winding roads that in the sixteenth century would have been little more than pack trails. The monastery there had been struggling for centuries with fires, conflicts, and chronic shortage of funds. In 1430, a devastating fire had nearly ended the foundation, and for decades afterward the canons lived more in their Freiburg properties than in the mountain house.

What would draw a man from the Calw region to Sankt Märgen? The monastery, for all its troubles, still controlled extensive forest lands and still needed men to manage them. A family with administrative experience and noble credentials, even minor ones, might find opportunities there that Calw could not offer. His death in Sankt Märgen in 1551 places him there in the middle years of the sixteenth century, exactly when the monastery was beginning a slow recovery.

But Hans Peter "Mang" von Braun left no direct descendants in our line. The connection forward runs through his relatives, through cousins or nephews whose exact relationship the records do not preserve. By 1551, however, some members of the Braun family had established themselves in the deepest parts of the Black Forest, in the monastery lands where the next crucial piece of our story would unfold.

The Ruff Craftsmen

While the Brauns were making their way into the Black Forest from the west, another family was already established in the high country. The surname Ruff, with its many spelling variations (Ruef, Ruf, Rueff), appears in the records of Furtwangen and Gütenbach from the early 1500s onward, marking a lineage of craftsmen whose skills would eventually merge with the Braun line.

Thomas Ruf was born in 1529 in Gütenbach, a tiny settlement in a side valley off the main ridge. Gütenbach would have been perhaps thirty or forty farmsteads scattered along a stream, each one combining subsistence agriculture with some form of craft production. The growing season at this elevation was barely four months. The soil was thin and rocky. No family could live on farming alone. So they made things. In winter, when the snow lay deep and the cattle stood in their stalls, the men worked at benches and lathes, shaping wood into useful objects: spoons and bowls, shuttles for looms, spindles for thread, parts for wagons and plows. And increasingly, as the decades passed, clocks.

Furtwangen in 1808 — The clockmaking center where the Ruff family worked their craft for generations

Thomas Ruf married Rosina Seng in 1554. She too was from Gütenbach, daughter of another craftsman family. Their marriage represents the normal pattern of these high forest communities: endogamy, marriage within the same tight circle of families who shared the same skills and faced the same challenges. The Ruf and Seng and Dilger and Faller names appear again and again in the Gütenbach records, interweaving through marriages and godparentage and business partnerships. The Ruf family continued in Furtwangen and Gütenbach through the late 1500s and into the 1600s, and it was their descendants who would connect to the monastery families down the ridge in Sankt Märgen and Sankt Peter.

The Schwarzwälder Uhr, the Black Forest clock, has its origins in the seventeenth century, but the groundwork was laid earlier. By the 1520s, craftsmen in the high Black Forest were experimenting with wooden gear trains and weight-driven mechanisms. The first true Black Forest clocks, all-wooden timepieces with simple movements and painted shields, appeared around 1640. By 1700, clock making had become a major industry, with raw movements produced in high valley workshops and carried to the lowlands by traveling peddlers who sold them across Europe. The culture of precision woodworking that made those clocks possible — the carving of gears, the turning of spindles, the patient development of techniques passed from father to son — had been developing for a century before the first clock was made. The Ruff family, working in Gütenbach and Furtwangen from the 1520s onward, were part of that foundational generation.

The Monastery Nexus

Sankt Märgen burned in 1430, again in 1560, and again in 1704. Each time, the monastery had to be rebuilt, and each rebuilding required vast quantities of timber and skilled labor. Between fires there were conflicts with Freiburg over rights and revenues, conflicts with the abbey's secular protectors over jurisdictions, chronic problems with discipline among the canons. But through all this trouble, the monastery endured, and the community around it endured, because there was nowhere else to go. The high Black Forest offered few choices: you worked with the monastery, or you left for the lowlands.

Sankt Märgen and Sankt Peter sat on adjacent ridges separated by only a few kilometers as the crow flies, but by a steep valley in between that made the journey a half-day's walk. Both monasteries controlled extensive forests. Both served as social centers for the scattered settlements around them, holding markets, providing the only schools for miles, serving as courts for settling disputes, maintaining the only decent roads through the mountains. Sankt Peter, founded as a Benedictine house by the Dukes of Zähringen in the 1090s, had been their family monastery and burial church. The baroque church that tourists now visit was built in the 1720s, but it replaced structures going back to the original Romanesque building. Each iteration required craftsmen, woodworkers, stonecutters — all the trades that a major construction project demanded.

Into this world of monastery work and mountain craftsmanship came the families whose names run through our genealogy: Braun from the west through Calw and Calmbach, Ruff from the south through Furtwangen and Gütenbach, and others whose origins we can less clearly trace. Steurenthaler, Dilger, Gschwander, Schurhammer. Swiss-German surnames, many of them, suggesting migration from the cantons across the Rhine where landless younger sons sought their fortunes in the German mountains. They spoke Alemannic German, a dialect nearly impenetrable to outsiders, that had evolved in the isolated valleys into forms differing from settlement to settlement. The monasteries, conducting their business in Latin and High German, served as the only linguistic bridges to the wider world.

Sankt Peter monastery — The Benedictine house where Christianus Schwär was born, lived, and died

The Critical Marriage

All of these lineages, these movements of people and families across the Black Forest landscape over 150 years, converge in a single marriage that took place sometime in the mid-1600s. The groom was Christianus Schwär, born around 1615 in Sankt Peter. The bride was Christina Rueff, born around 1638 in Oberibental, a hamlet in the Sankt Märgen district.

This marriage is the hinge on which our entire family history turns. Through Christianus Schwär, we trace back to the Sankt Peter monastery families and eventually, through several generations of poorly documented connections, to the Braun line from Ottobeuren. Through Christina Rueff, we trace back to Thomas Ruf and Rosina Seng of Gütenbach, to the craftsmen of the high forest. Their union brought together two centuries of separate family histories that had been slowly converging through the complex geography of the Black Forest.

Christianus lived his entire life in and around Sankt Peter. The Schwär family (the spelling varies: Schwär, Schwer, Schwehr) were established in the Sankt Peter lands, likely holding some minor administrative or craft position in the monastery's economy. Christianus married twice. His first wife was Margareta Kaltenbach, born 1614 in Sankt Peter, who died in 1656. After Margareta's death, Christianus married Christina Rueff.

Christina came from Oberibental, a cluster of farms in a high valley on the Sankt Märgen side of the ridge. The name literally means "upper Iben valley," Iben being a species of tree. This was wild country, forest and pasture land, the kind of place where a family might have a few cows, a garden plot, and a workshop where the men turned wood on winter evenings. Through Christina, the practical knowledge of woodworking families — the hands that could shape a gear wheel from a block of pear wood, that understood grain and tension and the behavior of timber in cold and heat — entered the Schwär line. Whether this craft heritage passed directly to subsequent generations, or simply became part of the family's identity as people who made things with their hands, we cannot say with certainty. But the skills of the Ruff woodworkers would find echoes in later generations, in the carpentry and joinery that other branches of the family would practice.

Christianus and Christina's marriage produced thirteen children. Thirteen, in an age when perhaps half of all children died before their fifth birthday. That most of them apparently survived to adulthood is itself remarkable, and it speaks to something about the household — perhaps Christina's competence, perhaps the relative security of the Sankt Peter monastery economy, perhaps simple genetic fortune. A household of fifteen would have filled one of the massive Black Forest farmhouses to capacity: the great timber structure perhaps sixty feet long and forty wide, with the cattle stalled below and the hayloft towering above, the family occupying the middle floor around the ceramic Kachelofen that held warmth for hours after the fire died. Feeding that many mouths required every resource available — rye bread and oat porridge, milk and cheese from the cows below, cabbages and turnips from the garden, whatever the forest could provide. These children, born between roughly 1660 and 1680, spread across the high Black Forest, marrying into other families, carrying forward both the Schwär name and the mixed heritage of monastery connections and craft traditions.

Christianus Schwär died on July 12, 1692, in Sankt Peter. He was buried the same day, as was customary in summer. He had lived seventy-seven years, an exceptional age for the seventeenth century. He had survived the Thirty Years War, which raged through Germany from 1618 to 1648 and may have reduced the Black Forest's population by a third. He had married twice, fathered at least thirteen children, seen at least some of them marry and produce grandchildren. He had lived his entire life within a radius of perhaps twenty kilometers, probably never traveling farther from Sankt Peter than a long day's walk could carry him. Yet his life connected families and traditions that had been separate for two hundred years.

The seventeenth century in the Black Forest was dominated by the Thirty Years War and its aftermath. From 1618 to 1648, German-speaking lands were devastated by a conflict that began as a religious war and evolved into a European-wide struggle for power. The Black Forest, lying along the routes between the Rhine valley and the Danube basin, saw repeated passages of armies. Swedish, French, Spanish, and Imperial troops all marched through, living off the land and leaving devastation behind. Monastery lands like Sankt Märgen and Sankt Peter were theoretically protected by their ecclesiastical status, but in practice soldiers respected little. Recovery was slow, taking generations. Yet people endured, families continued, children were born and raised. Christianus Schwär's thirteen children represented a kind of demographic recovery, a reassertion of life in the face of death.

The Meaning of the Migration

Through Christianus and Christina's children, the line would eventually reach the Gschwander family in Glottertal, another valley system north of Sankt Peter, and through the Gschwanders to the Schwehr family (the spelling had evolved by this point) of Heuweiler, a village in the foothills where the mountains gave way to the Rhine plain. The records between Christianus's death in 1692 and the birth of Afra Schwehr in 1857 are incomplete — fires destroyed some, the Napoleonic dissolution of the monasteries scattered others. We can trace the line of descent but not the full story of each generation. What we can see is a gradual movement downhill: from the ridgetops of Sankt Peter and Sankt Märgen to the somewhat lower and more hospitable Glottertal, with its terraced vineyards on south-facing slopes, and finally to Heuweiler, warmer and more connected to the market economy of the lowlands and the city of Freiburg.

Looking back across these two and a half centuries, from 1450 to 1700, a pattern emerges that might not have been visible to the people living through it. The von Braun family left the security and structure of Ottobeuren for the uncertainties of the Black Forest. In doing so, they gave up some things. The "von" gradually disappeared from most lines, as noble status meant little in mountain valleys where everyone worked with their hands. The connection to great institutions like Ottobeuren Abbey was replaced by connections to lesser houses. The relatively comfortable life of Swabian agricultural estates gave way to the hard subsistence of mountain farming and craft work.

But they gained things too. Independence, for one. In the Black Forest, far from the concentrated populations and rigid hierarchies of the lowlands, a family had more room to maneuver. Skills, for another. The merger with the Ruff craftsmen brought practical abilities that would prove valuable through changing circumstances. And adaptability, perhaps most important. A family that had survived the transition from Swabian nobility to Black Forest craftsmen had demonstrated a capacity to reinvent itself, to find new roles when old ones became untenable.

By 1700, they were part of a dense network of interrelated families who made things with their hands and endured on land that was beautiful and harsh in equal measure. The next century would bring new challenges and new migrations, as political upheaval and economic change swept through the German lands. But for now, at the turn of the eighteenth century, they were settled. The forest had claimed them, shaped them, made them its own.

And somewhere in the genealogical web that connected Sankt Peter to Glottertal to Heuweiler, the line was moving toward a woman named Afra, who would marry a man named Georg Gehri and give birth to a daughter named Anna Luise. But that story belongs to the next chapter.
