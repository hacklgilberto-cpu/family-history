Part Two: Into the Forest
The Black Forest Migration and the Craftsmen
1450–1700
When Blasius and Adam von Braun left Ottobeuren sometime in the middle years of the fifteenth century, they turned their faces westward toward the mountains that the Romans had called Silva Nigra, the Black Forest. It was a landscape utterly unlike the gentle Swabian farmland of their birth, a country of steep valleys and dark ridges where spruce and fir grew so thick that the forest floor remained in perpetual twilight even at midday. Here the streams ran swift and cold over granite boulders. Here the winters brought snow that might lie six feet deep from November to April. Here the old gods of the Germanic tribes had lingered longer than in the civilized lowlands, and the forests still harbored wolves and bears and wild boar. But here too were opportunities that Ottobeuren could no longer offer, for the Black Forest in the mid-fifteenth century was a frontier, a place where men with skill and ambition could carve out new lives far from the restrictive hierarchies of the monastic lands.

The Swabian Alb — The gentle rolling landscape the Braun family left behind when they turned westward toward the mountains

The Geography of Opportunity

The Black Forest was not a single entity but a series of parallel mountain ridges running roughly north to south, separated by deep valleys where the tributaries of the Rhine and the Danube found their sources. The northern end of the range lay not far west of Ottobeuren, rising abruptly from the Swabian plain. Here the land was lower, the valleys broader, and settlement had come early. Towns like Calw and Pforzheim had existed since Roman times, and by the fifteenth century they were prosperous centers of craft and trade.

Calw in particular had become known for its woodworking guilds. The town sat on the river Nagold, which provided waterpower for mills and transport for finished goods. The surrounding forests offered timber in seemingly inexhaustible quantities. Oak for structural work, beech for furniture, pine and fir for everything from roof shingles to barrel staves. The craft guilds were old and well organized, with strict rules about apprenticeship and quality, but they were also pragmatic. The population losses of the plague years meant that skilled craftsmen could find work, and families with some resources could establish themselves if they were willing to learn the trade.

South of Calw, the forest grew higher and wilder. The central Black Forest reached elevations of over three thousand feet, with peaks rising even higher. Here the valleys narrowed to gorges, and the settlements clung to whatever level ground could be found along the streams. This was mining country, where men dug for silver and lead and iron in the mountain flanks, and where the need for timber to shore up mine shafts and smelt ore created constant demand for woodcutters and charcoal burners.

Deeper still into the mountains lay the monastery lands. Sankt Märgen and Sankt Peter, two Benedictine foundations that mirrored in the Black Forest what Ottobeuren had been in Swabia, stood on adjacent ridges in the high country south of Freiburg. Sankt Märgen had been founded around 1118 as an Augustinian foundation, though it would later affiliate with other houses. Sankt Peter, established in the 1090s by the Dukes of Zähringen, was purely Benedictine. Both monasteries held extensive lands, both needed administrators and craftsmen, both offered the kind of structured, hierarchical world that a family like the Brauns would have understood from their Ottobeuren origins.

The Black Forest in the fifteenth century was undergoing rapid development. Population was recovering from the plague, and new technologies were driving expansion. Water-powered mills became more efficient, allowing for mechanized sawing of timber and crushing of ore. The demand for wood products grew with every passing decade. By 1500, the forests around Calw and Pforzheim were being systematically harvested, with regulations to ensure sustainable cutting. The high Black Forest, more remote and difficult of access, remained largely untouched except around the monasteries and mining camps. It was a landscape that rewarded those willing to work hard in harsh conditions, offering in exchange a degree of independence that was increasingly rare in the more settled parts of Germany.

Calw on the Nagold — The woodworking center where the Braun family likely first settled in the Black Forest

The Calw Connection

The trail grows cold in the records between Ottobeuren and the Black Forest. We know that Blasius von Braun lived until 1510 and his brother Adam Luppert von Braun until 1499. We know that one or both of them had sons who continued the family line. We know that by the early sixteenth century, members of the Braun family were established in the Calw region, because we can trace their descendants there. But the exact path they took, the exact year they left, the reasons they chose the Black Forest over other destinations, these are lost to time.

What we can reconstruct is the world they would have entered. Calw in the mid to late 1450s was a town of perhaps two thousand souls, clustered along both banks of the Nagold in a valley where the river made a convenient ford. The town had walls and gates, a market square, several churches, a ducal castle on the heights above. The guilds controlled most economic activity. To work as a master craftsman required years of apprenticeship, journeyman wandering, and finally the production of a masterpiece to prove one's skill. But the guilds also recognized nobility, and a family bearing the "von" designation would have had certain advantages in negotiating entry into the craft economy.

Calmbach, a smaller settlement a few kilometers upstream from Calw, appears in the family records as a place of residence for Adolph von Braun and his wife Catharina Lauxen, both born around 1485. Whether this Adolph was a grandson of the brothers who left Ottobeuren, or perhaps a nephew, we cannot say with certainty. But the "von" remains in his name, suggesting that the family still claimed noble status, and the Lauxen connection indicates local ties being formed through marriage.

Calmbach was primarily a woodworking center, known particularly for the production of roof shingles and barrel components. The steep hillsides around the settlement made farming difficult but provided easy access to standing timber. Men would fell trees on the slopes above the town, let the logs slide downhill to the river, and float them downstream to the sawmills. It was dangerous work. Every year men were crushed by rolling logs or drowned in the swift current trying to guide timber rafts through rapids. But it was also profitable work for those who survived, and it required skills that could be learned through practice rather than formal apprenticeship.

The Black Forest — Dense spruce and fir forests where winter brought snow six feet deep and summers were brief

Into the High Forest

The next clear point in the family geography comes from a single name and place: Hans Peter "Mang" von Braun, born 1505 in Haiterbach, died 1551 in Sankt Märgen. This man represents the family's deepest penetration into the Black Forest interior, and his death in a monastery town suggests connections that would prove crucial for understanding how the Braun line eventually merged with other families.

Haiterbach lies northwest of Calw, still in the lower reaches of the forest. But Sankt Märgen sits at nearly nine hundred meters elevation on a ridge in the high Black Forest, accessible only by steep and winding roads that in the sixteenth century would have been little more than pack trails. The monastery there had been struggling for centuries with fires, conflicts with its secular overlords, and chronic shortage of funds. In 1430, a devastating fire had nearly ended the foundation, and for decades afterward the canons lived more in their Freiburg properties than in the mountain house.

What would draw a man from the Calw region to Sankt Märgen in the early to mid-1500s? The monastery, for all its troubles, still controlled extensive forest lands and still needed men to manage them. A family with administrative experience and noble credentials, even minor ones, might find opportunities there that Calw could not offer. The "Mang" byname is intriguing, possibly a diminutive or nickname whose meaning has been lost. His death in Sankt Märgen in 1551 places him there in the middle years of the sixteenth century, exactly when the monastery was beginning a slow recovery under more energetic leadership.

But Hans Peter "Mang" von Braun left no direct descendants in our line. The connection forward runs instead through his relatives, through cousins or nephews whose exact relationship the records do not preserve. What matters is that by 1551, some members of the Braun family had established themselves in the deepest parts of the Black Forest, in the monastery lands where the next crucial piece of our story would unfold.

The Ruff Craftsmen

While the Brauns were making their way into the Black Forest from the west, another family was already established in the high country, working a trade that would define the region for centuries to come. The surname Ruff, with its many spelling variations (Ruef, Ruf, Rueff), appears in the records of Furtwangen and Gütenbach from the early 1500s onward, marking a lineage of craftsmen whose skills would eventually merge with the Braun nobility.

Thomas Ruf was born in 1529 in Gütenbach, a tiny settlement in a side valley off the main ridge where Furtwangen sat. Gütenbach in 1529 would have been perhaps thirty or forty farmsteads scattered along a stream, each one combining subsistence agriculture with some form of craft production. The growing season at this elevation was short, barely four months between the last frost of spring and the first frost of autumn. The soil was thin and rocky, scraped from the hillsides by generations of peasants terracing every slope that could hold a plow. No family could live on farming alone.

So they made things. In winter, when the snow lay deep and the cattle stood in their stalls munching hay cut the previous summer, the men of Gütenbach and Furtwangen worked at benches and lathes, shaping wood into useful objects. Spoons and bowls, shuttles for looms, spindles for thread, parts for wagons and plows. But especially clocks.

Furtwangen in 1808 — The clockmaking center where the Ruff family worked their craft for generations

The Schwarzwälder Uhr, the Black Forest clock, has its origins in the seventeenth century, but the groundwork was laid earlier. By the 1520s and 1530s, craftsmen in the high Black Forest were already experimenting with wooden gear trains and weight-driven mechanisms, adapting the principles of tower clocks to domestic scale. The first true Black Forest clocks, all-wooden timepieces with simple movements and painted shields, appeared around 1640. But the culture of precision woodworking that made those clocks possible had been developing for a century before that. The Ruff family, working in Gütenbach and Furtwangen from the 1520s onward, were part of that foundational generation. They carved the gears, turned the spindles, developed the techniques that their grandsons and great-grandsons would use to create an industry that would make the Black Forest famous across Europe.

Thomas Ruf married Rosina Seng in 1554. She too was from Gütenbach, born in 1533, daughter of another craftsman family. Their marriage represents the normal pattern of these high forest communities: endogamy, marriage within the same tight circle of families who shared the same skills and faced the same challenges. Everyone was related to everyone else within a few generations. The Ruf and Seng and Dilger and Faller names appear again and again in the Gütenbach records, interweaving through marriages and godparentage and business partnerships.

The Ruf family continued in Furtwangen and Gütenbach through the late 1500s and into the 1600s. They were woodworkers, clockmakers, sometimes farmers, always inhabitants of that high, harsh, beautiful country where winter lasted eight months and summer was a brief explosion of green before the first snows returned. And it was their descendants, generations later, who would connect to the monastery families down the ridge in Sankt Märgen and Sankt Peter.

The Monastery Nexus

The key to understanding how these families merged lies in the peculiar geography and economy of the high Black Forest in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries. Sankt Märgen and Sankt Peter sat on adjacent ridges separated by only a few kilometers as the crow flies, but by a steep valley in between that made the journey between them a half-day's walk. Both monasteries controlled extensive forests. Both needed constant supplies of timber for construction, firewood for heating, charcoal for metalworking. Both employed foresters and woodcutters and craftsmen to manage and exploit these resources.

The monasteries also served as social centers for the scattered settlements around them. They held markets, they provided the only schools for miles around, they served as courts for settling disputes, they maintained the only decent roads through the mountains. If you were born within twenty kilometers of Sankt Märgen or Sankt Peter in the 1500s or 1600s, your life was oriented toward one or both of those houses, whether you were a canon in the cloister or a farmer in a valley hamlet.

Sankt Märgen had a particularly troubled history. The great fire of 1430 was followed by more fires in 1560 and 1704. Each time, the monastery had to be rebuilt, and each rebuilding required vast quantities of timber and skilled labor. Between fires, there were conflicts with the town of Freiburg over rights and revenues, conflicts with the abbey's secular protectors over jurisdictions, chronic problems with discipline among the canons. But through all this trouble, the monastery endured, and the community around it endured, because there was nowhere else to go. The high Black Forest offered few choices: you worked with the monastery, or you left for the lowlands.

Sankt Peter, though less catastrophically afflicted, faced similar challenges. Founded as a Benedictine house by the Dukes of Zähringen in the 1090s, it had been their family monastery and burial church. By the sixteenth century, the Zähringer line was long extinct, but Sankt Peter continued as a center of learning and landholding. The baroque church that tourists now visit was built in the 1720s, but it replaced an earlier Gothic structure that itself replaced the original Romanesque building. Each iteration required craftsmen, woodworkers, stonecutters, glaziers, and all the other trades that a major construction project demanded.

Into this world of monastery work and mountain craftsmanship came the families whose names run through our genealogy: Braun from the west through Calw and Calmbach, Ruff from the south through Furtwangen and Gütenbach, and others whose origins we can less clearly trace but whose presence in the monastery lands is well documented. Steurenthaler, Dilger, Gschwander, Schurhammer. Swiss-German surnames, many of them, suggesting migration from the south and east, from the cantons across the Rhine where landless younger sons sought their fortunes in the German mountains.

The Critical Marriage

All of these lineages, these movements of people and families across the Black Forest landscape over 150 years, converge in a single marriage that took place sometime in the mid-1600s. The groom was Christianus Schwär, born around 1615 in Sankt Peter. The bride was Christina Rueff, born around 1638 in Oberibental, a hamlet in the Sankt Märgen district.

This marriage is the hinge on which our entire family history turns. Through Christianus Schwär, we trace back to the Sankt Peter monastery families and eventually, through several generations of poorly documented connections, to the Braun line from Ottobeuren. Through Christina Rueff, we trace back to Thomas Ruf and Rosina Seng of Gütenbach, to the craftsmen and clockmakers of the high forest. Their union brought together nobility and craftsmanship, monastery administration and mountain trades, two centuries of separate family histories that had been slowly converging through the complex geography of the Black Forest.

Christianus Schwär lived his entire life in and around Sankt Peter. He was born there around 1615, probably baptized in the monastery church, raised in the shadow of the great house. The Schwär family (the spelling varies: Schwär, Schwer, Schwehr) were established in the Sankt Peter lands, likely holding some minor administrative or craft position in the monastery's economy. Christianus married twice. His first wife was Margareta Kaltenbach, born 1614 in Sankt Peter, who died in 1656. With her he had children whose names have not survived. After Margareta's death, Christianus married Christina Rueff.

Christina came from Oberibental, a cluster of farms in a high valley on the Sankt Märgen side of the ridge. The name literally means "upper Iben valley," Iben being a species of tree. This was wild country, forest and pasture land, the kind of place where a family might have a few cows, a garden plot, and a workshop where the men turned wood on winter evenings. The Rueff family there traced back to the Furtwangen/Gütenbach craftsmen, though the exact links through the generations remain unclear. What matters is that Christina brought with her the skills and connections of the woodworking tradition, the practical knowledge of men who made things with their hands.

Christianus and Christina's marriage produced thirteen children. Thirteen. In an age of high infant mortality, this was an astonishing number, and it suggests that most of them survived to adulthood. These children, born between roughly 1660 and 1680, spread across the high Black Forest, marrying into other families, carrying forward both the Schwär name and the mixed heritage of monastery connections and craft traditions. Through them, the line would eventually reach the Gschwander family in Glottertal, another valley system north of Sankt Peter, and through the Gschwanders to the Schwehr family of Heuweiler, and through the Schwehrs finally to Afra Schwehr in 1857, whose life we will examine in later chapters.

Christianus Schwär died on July 12, 1692, in Sankt Peter. He was buried the same day in the monastery graveyard, as was customary in an age before embalming, when bodies could not be kept long in summer heat. He had lived seventy-seven years, an exceptional age for the seventeenth century. He had survived childhood diseases, the Thirty Years War (which raged through Germany from 1618 to 1648, certainly affecting the Black Forest region), multiple epidemics, the famines and crop failures that struck periodically in the high mountains. He had married twice, fathered at least thirteen children, seen at least some of them marry and produce grandchildren. He had lived his entire life within a radius of perhaps twenty kilometers, probably never traveling farther from Sankt Peter than a long day's walk could carry him. Yet his life connected families and traditions that had been separate for two hundred years.

The seventeenth century in the Black Forest was dominated by the Thirty Years War and its aftermath. From 1618 to 1648, German-speaking lands were devastated by a conflict that began as a religious war between Catholic and Protestant princes and evolved into a European-wide struggle for power. The Black Forest, lying along the routes between the Rhine valley and the Danube basin, saw repeated passages of armies. Swedish, French, Spanish, and Imperial troops all marched through at various times, living off the land and leaving devastation in their wake. Monastery lands like Sankt Märgen and Sankt Peter were theoretically protected by their ecclesiastical status, but in practice soldiers respected little. Sankt Märgen in particular suffered greatly, with its buildings occupied, its forests cut for firewood, its grain stores requisitioned. The population of the Black Forest region may have fallen by as much as a third during the war years. Recovery was slow, taking generations. Yet people endured, families continued, children were born and raised. Christianus Schwär's long life, spanning 1615 to 1692, encompassed the entire war period and the difficult reconstruction that followed. His thirteen children represented a kind of demographic recovery, a reassertion of life in the face of death.

The World of the High Forest

What was life actually like for families like the Schwärs and Rueffs in the Black Forest of the 1600s? We can reconstruct it from scattered sources, from the inventories that sometimes survive when estates were settled, from court records when disputes arose, from the careful accounts that monastery administrators kept of their tenants and workers.

Houses were built of timber, of course, since timber was the one resource the forest provided in abundance. The typical Black Forest house of this period was a massive structure, often housing both people and animals under one roof. The ground floor might be a stone foundation containing stalls for cows and storage for hay and tools. Above this, reached by an exterior stair, was the living floor: a large heated room where the family cooked, ate, worked, and slept; perhaps a separate chamber for the master and mistress; storage areas for grain and preserved foods. Above this again was the hayloft, filling the enormous attic space under the pitched roof. The whole structure might be sixty feet long and forty feet wide, with walls two feet thick and a roof extending far out beyond the walls to protect the timber from rain.

Heat came from a large ceramic stove, built into the wall between the main room and the sleeping chamber so that it could warm both spaces. These stoves, called Kachelofen in German, were works of art, built up from shaped ceramic tiles that radiated heat evenly and held warmth for hours after the fire died. Fuel was wood, of course, and a family might burn several cords over the course of a winter, but this was one expense they could meet from their own labor, cutting and splitting and stacking timber from the common forest or from their own woodlot.

Food was simple and seasonal. Bread from rye or oats, porridge from barley or buckwheat, dairy products from the family cow, eggs from chickens, vegetables from the garden in summer, preserved cabbage and turnips in winter. Meat was a rarity for most families, reserved for feast days and weddings. The forests provided some supplemental food: mushrooms and berries in autumn, game for those with hunting rights. But the growing season was short, the soils were poor, and every family lived close to the edge of hunger. A single crop failure could mean disaster.

So they worked at other things to supplement their farming. They wove linen from flax they grew themselves. They made wooden implements and sold them in the lowland markets. And increasingly, as the seventeenth century wore on, they made clocks. The first true Black Forest clocks appeared around 1640, simple wooden mechanisms with a single hand and a painted wooden face. By 1700, clock making had become a major industry, with raw clock movements being produced in the high valley workshops and then carried to the lowlands by traveling peddlers who sold them across Europe. The Rueff family was part of this transformation, and the skills passed down through Christina to her children and grandchildren.

Language and Culture

The people of the Black Forest in this era spoke a dialect of German that was nearly impenetrable to outsiders. Alemannic German, the linguistic tradition of southwestern Germany, Switzerland, and Alsace, had evolved in the isolated mountain valleys into forms that differed from valley to valley. A person from Sankt Märgen might struggle to understand someone from a village twenty kilometers away. The monasteries, conducting their business in Latin and High German, served as linguistic bridges, places where the standardized language of administration and learning could be encountered.

They were Catholic, universally and unquestioningly. The Reformation had touched the Black Forest lightly. A few towns in the northern reaches had gone Protestant, but the monastery lands remained firmly in the old faith. The rhythms of life followed the church calendar. Advent and Christmas, Lent and Easter, the feast days of local patron saints. These marked time more surely than any calendar. Work ceased on Sundays and holy days, and the village or hamlet gathered at the monastery church or the local chapel for Mass.

They were also, in some way that is hard to define from this distance, still connected to older traditions that predated Christianity. The forest itself was a presence, almost a character in their lives. There were places where you didn't go, trees you didn't cut, rituals for appeasing the forces that governed weather and harvest. The priests frowned on such things, called them superstition, but they persisted anyway, woven into the fabric of daily life in ways that left few traces in the written record.

The Generations Between

Between Christianus Schwär's death in 1692 and the birth of Afra Schwehr in 1857 lie 165 years and perhaps six or seven generations. We can trace some of them through the church records that the monasteries maintained, through the careful notations of births, marriages, and deaths that priests entered in leather-bound registers. But the records are incomplete. Fires destroyed some. Others were lost in the chaos of the Napoleonic era when the monasteries were dissolved and their archives scattered. What we have are fragments, enough to establish the line of descent but not enough to tell the full story of each generation.

What we know is that Christina Rueff's descendants stayed in the high Black Forest, in the territory around Sankt Märgen and Sankt Peter and the valleys that drained north toward Glottertal. Some of them surely continued the woodworking trades. Others farmed the mountain meadows, keeping a few cows, making cheese, living the hard life of the high country. Through intermarriage with other local families, the Schwär line connected to the Gschwanders, a Swiss-Alemannic family who had settled in Glottertal, probably in the late 1600s or early 1700s.

Glottertal church around 1890 — The valley where the Gschwander family settled and connected to the Schwär line

Glottertal, the Valley of the Glotter, was a slightly lower and more hospitable place than the ridge settlements. It had been settled earlier, and by the 1700s it had several substantial villages along the valley floor, with terraced vineyards on the south-facing slopes producing a thin acidic wine that the locals treasured. The valley drained west toward the Rhine plain and the city of Freiburg, making it more connected to the larger world than the isolated ridgetop hamlets.

From the Gschwanders, the line passed to the Schwehrs (the spelling had evolved by this point from Schwär to Schwehr) of Heuweiler, a village in the foothills where the mountains gave way to the Rhine plain. Heuweiler was lower still, warmer, more agricultural. Here the families were less isolated, more prosperous, more connected to the market economy of the lowlands. But they maintained their connections to the mountain families through marriage and kinship, and it was in Heuweiler that Afra Schwehr would be born in 1857, carrying in her blood and name the accumulated heritage of eight generations in the Black Forest.

The Meaning of the Migration

Looking back across these two and a half centuries, from 1450 to 1700, we can see a pattern that might not have been visible to the people living through it. The von Braun family left the security and structure of Ottobeuren for the uncertainties of the Black Forest. In doing so, they gave up some things. The "von" gradually disappeared from most lines, as noble status meant little in mountain valleys where everyone worked with their hands. The connection to great institutions like Ottobeuren Abbey was replaced by connections to lesser houses like Sankt Märgen and Sankt Peter. The relatively comfortable life of Swabian agricultural estates gave way to the hard subsistence of mountain farming and craft work.

But they gained things too. Independence, for one. In the Black Forest, far from the concentrated populations and rigid hierarchies of the lowlands, a family had more room to maneuver, more ability to make its own choices. Skills, for another. The merger with the Rueff craftsmen line brought practical abilities that would prove valuable through changing circumstances. Adaptability, perhaps most important. A family that had survived the transition from Swabian nobility to Black Forest craftsmen had demonstrated a capacity to reinvent itself, to find new roles when old ones became untenable.

And they had done something else, though they could not have known it at the time. They had positioned themselves on a frontier, in a region that would develop its own distinct economy and culture. The Black Forest in 1700 was on the verge of an industrial revolution of sorts, as clockmaking evolved from a cottage industry into a major export sector. The families who were already there, who had the skills and the connections, would be well placed to take advantage. The Rueff heritage, passed down through Christina to her children and grandchildren, would matter in ways that noble status from Ottobeuren never could.

By 1700, the family had been transformed. They were no longer the von Brauns of Ottobeuren but the Schwärs and Schwehrs of the Black Forest, part of a dense network of interrelated families who made things with their hands and endured on land that was beautiful and harsh in equal measure. The next century would bring new challenges and new migrations, as political upheaval and economic change swept through the German lands. But for now, at the turn of the eighteenth century, they were settled. The forest had claimed them, shaped them, made them its own.

And somewhere in the genealogical web that connected Sankt Peter to Glottertal to Heuweiler, the line was moving toward a woman named Afra, who would marry a man named Georg Gehri and give birth to a daughter named Anna Luise. But that story belongs to the next chapter.
