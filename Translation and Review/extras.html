The chapters of this history use only historical images, to keep the reader inside the period being described. But every place in this story still exists today. The abbey at Ottobeuren still stands. The valleys of the Glottertal still grow wine on their south-facing slopes. The house that Josef Würzburger built in Terra Nova still shelters a family. This page collects the places as they are now, alongside the migration map that traces the family's six-century journey from Swabia to Brazil and beyond.

The Places Today

The photographs below show the locations in this story as they appear in the twenty-first century. They are arranged by region, following the family's path from east to west across the German-speaking world, then across the Atlantic to Brazil and north to Canada.

Swabia: The Beginning

Ottobeuren Abbey and Basilica
Ottobeuren, Bavaria, Germany
The "fortress" of the title. The baroque basilica (1737-1766) stands on foundations dating to 764 CE. Four generations of the von Braun family lived in this abbey's shadow from 1352 to 1450.
Part I
Ottobeuren Village
Ottobeuren, Bavaria
The town that "clung to the abbey's walls like a child to its mother's skirts." Hans Peter Adam von Braun was married here in 1380.
Part I
The Unterallgäu Landscape
Between Memmingen and Mindelheim, Bavaria
The rolling Swabian farmland the Braun family left behind when they turned westward toward the mountains of the Black Forest.
Parts I & II
Reimlingen
Ries Basin, Bavaria, Germany
Birthplace of Hans Peter Adam von Braun in 1352. The earliest fixed point in the family geography.
Part I

The Black Forest: Into the Mountains

The Black Forest Highlands
Between Furtwangen and Sankt Peter, Baden-Württemberg
A traditional Black Forest farmhouse amid the high country where the Schwär and Rueff families lived for generations. Houses like these sheltered both people and livestock under one massive roof.
Part II
Calw on the Nagold
Calw, Baden-Württemberg, Germany
The woodworking centre where the Braun family likely first settled after leaving Swabia. The Nagold provided waterpower for the mills that drove the timber trade.
Part II
Calmbach
Bad Wildbad, Baden-Württemberg
Home of Adolph von Braun and Catharina Lauxen around 1485. A woodworking settlement where the family still carried the "von" particle marking noble status.
Part II
Furtwangen
Furtwangen im Schwarzwald, Baden-Württemberg
Centre of the Black Forest clockmaking tradition. The Ruff family worked here from the 1520s onward. Home of the German Clock Museum.
Part II
Gütenbach
Gütenbach, Baden-Württemberg
Birthplace of Thomas Ruf in 1529, the earliest documented Ruff craftsman. Christina Rueff, who married Christianus Schwär in the "critical marriage" of the story, traces back here.
Part II
Sankt Peter
Sankt Peter im Schwarzwald, Baden-Württemberg
The Benedictine monastery where Christianus Schwär was born, lived his entire life, fathered thirteen children, and was buried in 1692. The nexus of the Black Forest family network.
Parts II & III
Sankt Märgen
Sankt Märgen, Baden-Württemberg
Where Hans Peter "Mang" von Braun died in 1551. Christina Rueff's home territory. The other pole of the monastery network that connected the family lines.
Part II
Oberibental
Sankt Märgen district, Baden-Württemberg
Birthplace of Christina Rueff (c. 1638), whose marriage to Christianus Schwär united the Braun nobility with the Ruff craftsmen, the pivotal union of Part II.
Part II

The Valley: Glottertal and the Foothills

The Glottertal
Glottertal, Baden-Württemberg, Germany
The valley of the Glotter, stretching from the Rhine plain into the Black Forest. The Würzburger family has lived here since the 1730s. The Gschwander family settled here from Switzerland. Wine still grows on the south-facing slopes.
Parts III & V
Heuweiler
Heuweiler, Baden-Württemberg
The Schwehr family's home for seven generations, from Bartholomäus's arrival around 1717 through Afra's childhood. One of the smallest municipalities in Baden-Württemberg.
Parts II & III
Föhrental
Föhrental, Glottertal, Baden-Württemberg
Home of the Würzburgerhof, the family's named farmstead. Josef Würzburger grew up here before emigrating to Brazil in 1933. The most isolated of the four Glottertal villages.
Parts III & V
Unterglottertal
Unterglottertal, Glottertal, Baden-Württemberg
The lower village, where the valley opens to the Rhine plain. Birthplace of Josef Würzburger's father Kasimir in 1858. The highest vineyards in Germany climb these south-facing slopes.
Part III
Ohrensbach
Ohrensbach, Glottertal, Baden-Württemberg
Where Josef and Anna Luise married in 1919. Where Elisabeth was born in 1922. Where Anna Luise died at thirty-six in 1925. A place of beginnings and endings.
Parts III & V
Wildtal
Wildtal, near Gundelfingen, Baden-Württemberg
Birthplace of Anna Luise Gehri in 1889. Home of the Gehri family. Where Georg Schwehr died at thirty-eight in 1866.
Part III
Gundelfingen
Gundelfingen, Baden-Württemberg
Birthplace of Afra Schwehr in 1857, before her parents' formal marriage. Where Georg Schwehr and Theresia Ruth settled when Heuweiler had no more land to offer.
Part III
The Kandel
Kandel (1,241 m), Black Forest, Baden-Württemberg
The highest point visible from the Glottertal. The mountain that defined the valley's eastern wall for every generation who lived below it.
Part III
Freiburg Münster
Freiburg im Breisgau, Baden-Württemberg
The great cathedral that dominated the Rhine plain, visible from the Black Forest heights above. The regional capital that oriented the entire valley world.
Parts II & III

Styria: The Other Country

Sankt Anna am Aigen
Southeastern Styria, Austria
The wine-growing village where the Hackl family originated. Johan Hackl was born here in 1839. Leonhard and Aurelia married in the parish church in 1896. The landscape could not be more different from the Black Forest.
Part IV
Hochstraden
Sankt Anna am Aigen parish, Styria
Leonhard Hackl's birthplace in 1869, on the volcanic basalt ridge above the village. About 145 residents today. Vineyards grow on two-million-year-old lava.
Part IV
The Stradner Kogel
Southeastern Styria, Austria (609 m)
The highest point in southeastern Styria. Views stretch east toward Hungary and south toward Slovenia. The volcanic landmark that dominates the Hackl homeland.
Part IV
Graz: Jakomini District
6th District, Graz, Styria, Austria
The working-class neighbourhood where Leonhard raised twelve children at Pestalozzistraße 78. Dense apartment blocks, modest rents. Friedrich was born here in 1914.
Part IV
Pfarrkirche St. Josef
Jakomini, Graz, Austria
Where Friedrich Hackl was baptised on 29 January 1914, the day after his birth. The church had only been completed in 1908. The paint on the walls was barely dry.
Part IV
Graz Hauptbahnhof
Graz, Styria, Austria
Leonhard worked as a Dienstmann, a porter, meeting trains and carrying passengers' luggage. The station was likely one of his daily workplaces.
Part IV
Schöder
Murau district, Upper Styria, Austria
Birthplace of Aurelia Küschal in 1871, Leonhard's wife. A mountain village in the Mur valley, colder and higher than the wine country where Leonhard grew up.
Part IV

Brazil: The Crossing

Port of Santos
Santos, São Paulo, Brazil
Where the Hackl family disembarked from the SS Orania on 10 February 1925. Their first moment on Brazilian soil. Still the largest port in Latin America.
Part IV
Hospedaria dos Imigrantes
Mooca, São Paulo, Brazil (now Museu da Imigração)
The immigrant processing centre through which every newcomer to São Paulo state passed. Medical inspection, registration, temporary housing. The Hackl family was processed here in February 1925.
Part IV

Brazil: Paraná and São Paulo

Mandaguaçu
Mandaguaçu, Paraná, Brazil
Where the Hackl family settled and farmed. Where Friedrich and Elisabeth married in 1955. Where Frederico was born in 1957. Where Leonhard's grave lies in Brazilian soil, far from the churchyard at Sankt Anna am Aigen.
Parts IV & V
Maringá
Maringá, Paraná, Brazil
Where Leonhard Hackl died in 1953 and Aurelia in 1962. The conical Cathedral of Maringá, visible in this photograph, is where Gilberto Hackl and Eveline were later married, adding yet another generation's ceremony to the family story.
Parts IV & V
Colônia Terra Nova
~10 km from Castro, Paraná, Brazil
The German immigrant colony where Josef Würzburger built his house and raised his family. Still home to some sixty families descended from the original 116 who arrived in 1933. German traditions, festivals, and language persist here to this day.
Part V
The Iron Cross: Josef Würzburger's War
Imperial Germany, 1914–1918
Before he was a carpenter in Brazil, Josef Würzburger was a soldier. He fought in the First World War and was awarded the Iron Cross for his service. The man who later built a house in the Paraná highlands with his own hands had first survived the trenches of Europe.
Part V
São Paulo: Avenida Paulista
São Paulo, Brazil
Where Friedrich and Elisabeth eventually settled. Where Friedrich died in the 1990s. Where Gilberto was born in 1982. The Hospital Santa Catarina on the Avenida Paulista, visible here, is where Sabrina Hackl was born, another generation arriving in the city that absorbed the immigrant families.

Canada: The Circle Closes

Kitchener-Waterloo
Ontario, Canada
The city once called Berlin, where the Ruth brothers from Heuweiler settled in the 1850s. A century and a half later, Gilberto Hackl's son Michael was born here in 2020. The family's Canadian hometown and second home, closing a circle no one had planned.
Parts III & V (Epilogue)

Every place in this gallery is still inhabited, still serving the communities that grew up around them. The abbey bells still ring at Ottobeuren. The vineyards still climb the slopes of the Glottertal. And the house that Josef Würzburger built in Terra Nova with Araucaria pine and Black Forest joinery still stands, nearly a century after he raised its walls.
