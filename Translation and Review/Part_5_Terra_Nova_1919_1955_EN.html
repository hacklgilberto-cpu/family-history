Part Five: Terra Nova
The Carpenter's House and the Meeting of Two Lines
1919–1955
Two families, two countries, two journeys across the Atlantic. The Hackls from the volcanic hills of Styria, arriving at Santos on a Dutch steamship in February 1925, bound for the coffee frontier of northern Paraná. The Würzburgers from the vine-covered slopes of the Glottertal, arriving by a route that has been lost to the records, bound for a German colony on the cool highlands south of Curitiba. They did not know each other. They came from opposite ends of the German-speaking world. But Brazil, that vast and improbable country, would bring them together.

A Marriage in Ohrensbach

On the seventh of October, 1919, in the hamlet of Ohrensbach in the Glottertal, Josef Würzburger married Anna Luise Gehri. He was twenty-six, a Schreiner, a carpenter and joiner, the son of Kasimir Würzburger and Rosa Beck of Föhrental. She was thirty, born in Wildtal near Gundelfingen, the daughter of Georg Gehri and Afra Schwehr. Through Afra, Anna Luise carried in her blood the inheritance of the Schwehr line, reaching back through Heuweiler and Sankt Peter and, beyond that, to the von Braun nobility of Ottobeuren.

It was a marriage of two Glottertal families. The Würzburgers had been in Föhrental since the 1730s; the Gehri family was established in Wildtal, just a few kilometers away. It must have seemed, in that autumn of 1919, like a continuation of everything that had come before: another marriage in the same church, another entry in the same register, another generation taking its place in the ancient cycle of the valley.

The church in Glottertal — Where Josef Würzburger and Anna Luise Gehri married in 1919, and where Anna Luise was buried in 1925

But the world outside the Glottertal was in ruins. Germany had lost the war. The Kaiser had abdicated. The economy was shattered. Inflation was beginning its catastrophic rise. And in the Glottertal, the old problems persisted: too many people, too little land, too few ways to make a living. Josef Würzburger was a skilled craftsman, a Schreiner trained in the Black Forest tradition of precision woodworking, but skill alone did not guarantee survival in a world where the currency in your pocket lost half its value between breakfast and supper.

Anna Luise

Anna Luise Gehri was born on the fifteenth of May, 1889, in Wildtal. She grew up in a landscape of vineyards and small farms, in a family that had been touched by emigration on every side. Her Ruth uncles had gone to Canada. Her Schwehr uncle Georg had followed them to Waterloo, Ontario. The pull of the New World was a constant presence in the families of the Glottertal.

Anna Luise and Josef had their first years together in the Glottertal. Their daughter Maria Theresia Würzburger was born on the eighteenth of July, 1920, in Ohrensbach. Their son August Würzburger followed on the twenty-ninth of December, 1921, but lived only weeks, dying on the ninth of February, 1922. Then came Elisabeth Würzburger, born on the twenty-fourth of November, 1922, in Ohrensbach. Elisabeth. The girl who would one day marry Friedrich Hackl and become the grandmother of the person for whom this history is being written.

And then, on the thirtieth of June, 1925, Anna Luise died. She was thirty-six years old. The cause is not recorded, but the bare fact is devastating enough. Elisabeth was two years and seven months old. Maria Theresia was nearly five. Josef Würzburger was thirty-two, a widower with two small daughters in a country that offered him nothing.

Anna Luise was buried in Ohrensbach, in the same soil where her Würzburger and Gehri ancestors lay. She had lived her entire life within a radius of perhaps ten kilometers, from Wildtal where she was born to Ohrensbach where she died. She never saw Brazil. She never saw her daughter Elisabeth grow up. She exists in the family record as a name and a pair of dates: 1889–1925. But without her, without this woman who died young in a Black Forest hamlet, nothing that follows in this story would have happened. The line runs through Anna Luise like a river through a narrow gorge: briefly visible, quickly gone, but carrying everything forward.

The Second Marriage

Josef did not remain a widower long. He could not afford to. A man with two small children — the eldest not yet five, the youngest not yet three — needed a wife, and not for sentiment. Someone had to feed and wash and watch over those girls while he worked. There may have been an interval, perhaps weeks or months, when Maria Theresia and Elisabeth were cared for by neighbors or relatives, the improvised safety net that small communities stretched over their orphaned children. But the arrangement could not last.

On the twelfth of June, 1926, less than a year after Anna Luise's death, Josef married Maria Bühler. She was twenty-six, born on the twenty-third of January, 1900, in Ottenhöfen, the daughter of Andreas Bühler and Barbara Lenz. The speed of the remarriage was not callousness but necessity. The Glottertal in 1926, like the Heuweiler of a century earlier, was a place where marriage was as much a practical arrangement as an emotional bond.

Josef and Maria would have two children together: Anna Rosa Würzburger and Erich Würzburger. These half-siblings of Elisabeth's would later remain in Castro, Paraná, where they built their lives in the colony their father had helped to found. The family dynamics that this second marriage created — the division between the children of the first wife and the children of the second — would echo through the generations in ways both predictable and painful. Elisabeth, the daughter of the first marriage, would eventually find herself at a distance from the Würzburger family in Castro, a distance measured not in kilometers but in the quiet estrangements that blended families so often produce.

Maria Theresia, Josef and Anna Luise's eldest daughter, did not survive childhood. Born in 1920, she died on the twenty-fourth of June, 1932, in Ohrensbach, at the age of eleven. By the time Josef departed for Brazil in 1933, he had lost his first wife, his first son August, and his first daughter Maria Theresia. Of the three children he had with Anna Luise, only Elisabeth survived. She was ten years old when her half-sister died, and eleven when her father took the family across the Atlantic. What it meant to be that child — the sole survivor of her mother's line, carried to a new continent by a father who had remarried and started a second family — we can only imagine.

Terra Nova

In 1933, Josef Würzburger made his decision. He would leave Germany. The parish Familienblätter record it in a single line: Wanderte 1933 nach Brasilien, Staat Santa Catharina, Siedlung Terra Nova bei Castro. "Emigrated 1933 to Brazil, state of Santa Catarina, settlement Terra Nova near Castro." The state was recorded incorrectly, a clerical error that would persist. The settlement was not in Santa Catarina but in Paraná, in the municipality of Castro, one of the oldest towns in the state, founded in 1778 on the highland plateau known as the Campos Gerais.

On the eighteenth of July, 1933, the Fazenda Marilândia, a property of 1,300 alqueires about ten kilometers from Castro, was purchased by the Gesellschaft für Siedlung im Auslande, the Society for Colonization Abroad. One hundred and sixteen families received plots in the colony. Each family was given twelve alqueires, roughly twenty-nine hectares, of land divided into areas for cultivation, pasture, and settlement. They received a provisional house, seeds, and basic tools. The conditions were clear: you had to farm the land for at least two years. You had to build your own permanent house. You had to make it work.

For Josef Würzburger, a trained Schreiner, the requirement to farm was a challenge. He was a carpenter, not a farmer. But the colony needed carpenters perhaps more than it needed farmers, because before anyone could farm, someone had to build. Houses, barns, fences, tools, furniture: everything had to be constructed from scratch. A man who knew how to shape wood with precision, who had learned his craft in the Black Forest tradition of joinery, was worth his weight in seed corn.

How Josef and his family reached Brazil remains uncertain. Unlike the Hackl family, whose arrival on the SS Orania is documented, the Würzburger journey has left no trace in the immigrant records at the Museu da Imigração in São Paulo. This suggests they may have arrived at Paranaguá, the port on the Paraná coast, and taken the railroad up the Serra do Mar escarpment to Curitiba, bypassing São Paulo entirely. But this is speculation. The documents, if they exist, are in the hands of the family in Castro.

Castro, Paraná — One of the oldest cities in the state, near which Josef Würzburger built his home in the Colônia Terra Nova

The House He Built

Josef Würzburger, who now answered to the Portuguese spelling José, built his house in Terra Nova with his own hands. He used the timber of the region — Araucaria pine and imbuia and cedar — and he shaped it with the techniques he had brought from Germany, the mortise-and-tenon joints, the careful fitting of beam to beam, the precision that a Schreiner learned through years of apprenticeship in the Black Forest workshops. The house was not large, but it was solid. It was built to last.

And it has lasted. Nearly a century after it was raised, the house still stands in Terra Nova, its pure wood construction as robust as the day José fitted the last beam into place. The family eventually built a new, modern house directly in front of it, as families do when they outgrow the old structure but cannot bring themselves to tear it down. But José's house is still there, still usable, still used — a guest house now, sheltering visitors in rooms that a Black Forest carpenter built with hands trained in the Glottertal. The wood has darkened with age but has not weakened. The joints have not loosened. It is the most eloquent thing in this entire story, that house: a man's craft outliving the man himself, outliving the country he came from, outliving the language he spoke, standing in the Brazilian highlands as proof that some things, if they are made well enough, endure.

Castro itself sits at about a thousand meters above sea level on the Campos Gerais plateau, a landscape of rolling grasslands and Araucaria forests that bears a striking resemblance to parts of central Europe. The temperatures are mild, with cool winters that can bring frost. For German immigrants who had endured the brutal heat of the coffee frontier, the Paraná highlands must have felt like a homecoming. Not Germany, exactly, but close enough to breathe easily, to recognize the seasons.

Colônia Terra Nova today is a rural neighborhood about ten kilometers from downtown Castro, still home to some sixty families descended from the original 116 who arrived in 1933. Historic German-style houses with timber framing still stand along the colony's roads. The community maintains German traditions with remarkable tenacity: festivals featuring eisbein and braten mit soße, music and dancing, and a café colonial breakfast of broa, waffles, cakes, jams, and fresh dairy that would not be out of place in a Schwarzwald Gasthof. Some residents still speak German among themselves. It is, in its quiet way, a piece of the Glottertal transplanted to the Brazilian highlands, a living monument to the stubbornness of memory and the persistence of craft.

Elisabeth

Elisabeth Würzburger arrived in Brazil as a girl of ten or eleven, carried across the Atlantic by a father she shared with a stepmother and half-siblings. She had lost her mother at three, her sister Maria Theresia at ten, and now her country at eleven. Everything she had known — the Glottertal, the church at Ohrensbach, the grave where her mother lay — was gone, replaced by a colony in the Brazilian highlands where the language was strange and the future was uncertain.

She grew up in Terra Nova, in the house her father built. She learned Portuguese, the language her mother never spoke. She became Brazilian, as immigrant children do, absorbing the new country through its sounds and smells and tastes while the old country receded into the stories told at the dinner table in a German that grew more halting with each passing year.

Elisabeth was reserved and kind, unusually gentle by the standards of the German immigrant community, where bluntness was a virtue and sentiment was kept behind closed doors. She carried Germany with her in small rituals that her grandchildren would remember long after the reasons for them had been forgotten: the baking of gingerbreads at Christmas, following recipes no one had written down; the painting and hiding of Easter eggs in the garden, a tradition she performed with quiet care as though the act itself were a form of prayer. She was strict about food — a plate left unfinished was an insult, a sign of disrespect for what had been given — and she had a dry, private sense of humor that surfaced only in small ways. When she received a gift she did not like, she would hold onto it, sometimes for months, and present it to the original giver on their next birthday, her face perfectly composed, as if the coincidence were entirely innocent.

Friedrich and Elisabeth

And at some point, through a mechanism that adds one more layer of improbability to this story, Elisabeth met a young man named Friedrich Hackl. They did not meet by chance. According to family accounts, they were matched through a pairing service — a kind of matrimonial correspondence system, probably run through one of the German-language newspapers that circulated among immigrant communities in the Brazilian interior. Such services were common in a world where German-speaking settlers were scattered across thousands of kilometers of Paraná and São Paulo, in colonies and towns too far apart for young people to meet naturally. A notice would describe a man or woman's age, background, faith, and circumstances; interested parties would write back; and if the letters went well, a meeting would be arranged. It was courtship by post, practical and unsentimental, not so different from the arranged marriages of their medieval ancestors.

A Hackl from the tropical north of Paraná and a Würzburger from the temperate highlands in the south, brought together not by proximity but by the deliberate search for a partner who shared a language, a faith, and a cultural world.

On the third of December, 1955, in Mandaguaçu, Paraná, Friedrich Hackl married Elisabeth Würzburger. He was forty-one. She was thirty-three. Both had been born in the German-speaking lands of Europe and raised in the immigrant communities of Brazil. Both carried surnames that encoded the history of the families behind them: Hackl, the small axe, the tool of the working man; Würzburger, the name of a distant Franconian city that had followed a family across the ocean. With this marriage, two stories that had been running in parallel for more than five centuries finally converged. The fortress and the axe were joined at last.

Friedrich — who had been called Fritz in Graz and was now Frederico to everyone in Brazil — was a man shaped by the immigrant frontier. He had arrived at eleven, grown up on the coffee plantations of northern Paraná, and found his vocation not in farming but in gardening. He became a professional gardener, a man who loved flowers and growing things, who would later help plant the gardens of the Centro Empresarial in Santo Amaro, São Paulo. He had lost three fingers in a workplace accident, though his grandchildren would not learn this for years; instead, he would hold up his hand and tell them he could make fingers disappear by magic, and the children would stare in baffled wonder, unable to work out the trick, until an aunt finally explained what had really happened. He was rigid with his children in the way that men of his generation and background often were. His middle son, Frederico, was expected to hand over his entire paycheck when he came home from work, so the family could manage the money together. This was not cruelty but the inherited logic of scarcity: the family came first, and the family's needs were not negotiable.

Friedrich and Elisabeth settled first in Mandaguaçu, where they were pioneers in the settlement and became close friends with the Perissin family. Their first child, Aurelia Elisabeth, developed a severe form of muscular atrophy and arthritis that demanded medical treatment unavailable in the interior. The family made the difficult decision to move to São Paulo, selling or giving their land to the Perissins — who remained grateful for the gesture — and trading the open country of Paraná for the great metropolis.

Their son Frederico Hackl was born on the fifteenth of April, 1957, in Mandaguaçu. He would grow up in São Paulo, marry Solange de Melo Ribeiro, and father two sons. The elder, Gilberto Hackl, was born on the twentieth of December, 1982, in São Paulo. Gilberto would one day sit down at a computer and begin tracing these lines backward through the centuries, through the parish registers and baptismal certificates and ship manifests and Familienblätter, assembling the fragments of a story that no single ancestor had ever known in its entirety.

Friedrich and Elisabeth both died in São Paulo. Elisabeth went first, taken by stomach cancer — a slow, terrible illness that her son Frederico witnessed at close range, caring for her, accompanying her to the hospital, watching her die. It is a trauma the family avoids speaking about, and perhaps that silence explains why so few memories of Elisabeth have been shared across the generations. The grief was too close, too raw, and it was easier to say nothing than to say what it had been like. When she died, she was buried not in São Paulo but in Mandaguaçu, beside Leonhard and Aurelia Hackl — beside, perhaps, the family she had been searching for all her life, the belonging that a motherless girl carried across an ocean had finally found.

Friedrich outlived her. In his later years, alone, he was moved to a care home near the family's house. His grandchildren visited on weekends, sometimes bringing him home for lunch. One thing he never abandoned, even from a distance: the tending of the grave in Mandaguaçu where Leonhard and Aurelia and now Elisabeth lay. He found a way to care for it, always, as though the maintenance of that small plot of earth were the last obligation he could not put down. He died of a pulmonary embolism. He was buried not beside his parents and his wife but in Itapecerica da Serra, separated from them in death as he had not wished to be in life.

Of Josef's children from his second marriage, Anna Rosa Würzburger remained in Castro and raised her family there. Her brother Erich Würzburger, according to family memory, returned to Germany and became a Catholic priest — a detail that, if true, completes a remarkable arc: a Würzburger line that began in the shadow of the Black Forest monasteries, that left Germany for Brazil, and that sent one son back across the ocean to serve the same Church that had baptized his ancestors in the Glottertal for two hundred years.

Colônia Terra Nova, Castro — The German colony where Josef Würzburger built his house and raised his family, still maintaining its traditions ninety years later

Epilogue: The Circle

There is a final symmetry that no one planned. In the 1850s, the Ruth brothers of Heuweiler emigrated to the Waterloo region of Ontario, Canada. They settled in Woolwich and Elmira and the surrounding countryside, establishing a German-speaking community that would persist for generations. A century and a half later, Gilberto Hackl — great-great-great-grandson of the Schwehr and Ruth families of Heuweiler, descendant of the Würzburgers of Föhrental, grandson of Friedrich Hackl and Elisabeth Würzburger — moved to the Kitchener-Waterloo region of Ontario. His son Michael was born there, in 2020, in the same landscape where his distant Ruth cousins had put down roots in the 1850s.

Kitchener, Ontario — The city once called Berlin, where the Ruth brothers settled in the 1850s, and where Gilberto Hackl's son Michael was born in 2020

The circle closes. Not perfectly, not deliberately, but with the quiet insistence of a pattern that repeats across centuries. The same patch of Canadian soil that drew the Ruth brothers from the Black Forest in the age of sailing ships drew a Hackl from Brazil in the age of jet aircraft. The names had changed. The languages had shifted. The routes were different. But the destination was the same, as if the place itself had been waiting.

This is what a family history teaches us, if it teaches us anything at all. That the past is not past. That the decisions made by people whose names we barely know, in villages we have never visited, in centuries we can only imagine, echo forward through time in ways that are impossible to predict and impossible to ignore. Josef Würzburger, fitting a beam into place in the house he was building in Terra Nova, could not have known that someone would one day sleep in that house and feel, in the silence of the Paraná night, the presence of all the generations that had led to that moment.

The Hackl arms and the arms of Würzburg — The axe and the fortress, united at last in Brazil

But here we are. The fortress sheltered them. The axe shaped the world they built. And the story, which began with a child born during the Black Death in a Swabian monastery town, continues in São Paulo, in Kitchener, in whatever city or country or continent the next generation chooses to call home.

The valley does not hold them anymore. But it made them who they are.
