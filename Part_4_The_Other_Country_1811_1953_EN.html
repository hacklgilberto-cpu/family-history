Part Four: The Other Country
From the Volcanic Hills of Styria to the Forests of Paraná
1811–1953
Until now, this story has followed a single thread westward across the map of German-speaking Europe: from Ottobeuren to the Black Forest, from Sankt Peter to Heuweiler, from the monastery heights to the valley floor. But every family has more than one root, and the Hackl family enters this narrative from an entirely different direction, from the far southeastern corner of the Austrian lands, from a landscape of volcanic hills and vineyards that looks not toward the Rhine but toward Hungary and Slovenia. If the Schwehrs and Würzburgers were people of the Black Forest, shaped by spruce and granite and long winters, the Hackls were people of a gentler, warmer, more open country, a borderland where empires met and cultures mingled. Their story would carry them from those rolling Styrian hills to the second-largest city in Austria, and from there, on a Dutch steamship, across the Atlantic to a country none of them had ever seen.

Sankt Anna am Aigen — A historical postcard of the wine-growing village in southeastern Styria where the Hackl family originated

Volcanic Soil

Sankt Anna am Aigen sits on a ridge in the far southeastern corner of Styria, about seventy kilometers from Graz, in a landscape that could not be more different from the Black Forest. Where the Schwehr country was all narrow valleys and dark forests, this is open, rolling hill country, a terrain of long ridges and gentle slopes where vineyards climb every south-facing hillside and the views stretch east toward Hungary and south toward Slovenia. The village perches at about four hundred meters above sea level, with a church visible from far away on its hilltop, a landmark for travelers approaching from any direction.

The geology here is extraordinary. Two million years ago, volcanoes erupted across this part of Styria, leaving behind basalt formations and mineral-rich soils that would prove ideal for growing wine. The municipality is not a single settlement but a constellation of tiny hamlets scattered across the ridges: Waltra, Hochstraden, Sichauf, Klapping, Plesch, Risola, Jamm. Each is a handful of farmsteads clustered along a ridge or at a crossroads, a few dozen families at most, surrounded by vineyards and orchards on the volcanic slopes. The names appear in the parish registers almost interchangeably, the way Mandaguaçu and Maringá would later blur in Brazilian records: a community too small for its own registrar, folded into a larger parish that recorded all their births and deaths under a single church roof.

The area had been a borderland since Roman times, marking the frontier between the provinces of Noricum and Pannonia. By the nineteenth century, it sat at the meeting point of three political entities: the Austrian crownlands of Styria to the west, the Kingdom of Hungary to the east, and the lands that would eventually become Yugoslavia to the south. The people here spoke German, but Slovenian was heard in the markets, and Hungarian was the language of the administration across the eastern border. It was a place where worlds overlapped.

In the first three chapters of this history, the families of the Black Forest lived under the authority of the Grand Duchy of Baden, a small sovereign state in the fragmented German confederation. The Hackl family, by contrast, lived inside the Habsburg Empire, the sprawling multiethnic monarchy that stretched from the Swiss border to the Romanian plains and from the Adriatic coast to the Carpathian Mountains. The difference mattered enormously in daily life. In Baden, serfdom had been weakened early by Enlightenment reforms. In the Habsburg lands, formal serfdom persisted until the revolution of 1848. The Serfdom Patent issued by Emperor Joseph II in 1781 had granted peasants the right to marry freely and move between estates, but it left intact the system of corvée, the unpaid labor that tenants owed to their landlords. A farmer in the hamlets around Sankt Anna am Aigen in 1811, when Franz Hakel married Zäzilia Waltersdorfer, still owed days of labor on the lord's land every week, still surrendered a portion of his harvest as tithe. His counterpart in Heuweiler, three hundred kilometers to the west, had been free of such obligations for a generation. Both were poor. But they were poor under different legal systems, and those systems shaped the rhythms and possibilities of their lives in ways that the families themselves may never have articulated.

Franz and Zäzilia

The earliest Hackl we can name in the direct line is Franz Hakel. We know almost nothing about him: no birth date, no death date, no occupation, no parents. He appears in the record only once, on the eighteenth of February, 1811, when he married Zäzilia Waltersdorfer in the parish church of Sankt Anna am Aigen. That single entry, scratched in ink by a parish priest two centuries ago, is the earliest fixed point of the Hackl family's existence.

The surname itself is a clue. Hackl, sometimes written Hakl or Hakel depending on the hand of the registrar, derives from the Middle High German word for a small axe or hatchet, the kind of tool used for chopping wood, splitting kindling, or rough carpentry. It was an occupational name, like Zimmermann or Schreiner, given originally to a man who made or used such tools. The variant spellings reflect not different families but different scribes: a priest writing in careful Latin might record Hakel, while his successor, writing in German dialect, might put Hakl. Both referred to the same people, the same bloodline, the same house on the same volcanic ridge.

Franz and Zäzilia's children trace a pattern that reveals something about the family's life. Their first three children were baptized in Sankt Anna am Aigen: Michael in 1812, Josefus in 1814, Maria in 1817. But beginning with Theresia in 1821, the remaining four children were all recorded as born in Waltra: Anna in 1824, Johann in 1827, Franciscus in 1828. The shift likely reflects a move within the parish, from one hamlet to another, perhaps a few kilometers along the ridge, to a different plot of land. In the scattered settlement pattern of this region, where farmsteads stood alone on hillsides rather than clustered in villages, such moves were common and unremarkable. You inherited a plot or married into one, and your children were registered under whichever hamlet name the priest assigned to that particular piece of land.

Michael and Anna Nell

Franz and Zäzilia's eldest son, Michael Hakel, was born on the sixth of May, 1812, in Sankt Anna am Aigen. He married Anna Nell, born on the twelfth of September, 1814, also in the parish. The wedding took place on the twelfth of February, 1838, in the same church where Michael's parents had married twenty-seven years earlier. Anna's father, Franz Nell, had been born in 1789 in the parish and died there in 1815, when Anna was barely a year old. The Nell family, like the Hackls, had roots deep in this soil.

Michael and Anna produced ten children over twenty-one years, a family of extraordinary size even by the standards of the time. The parish registers record them methodically: Anna in 1837, Maria in 1838, Theresia in 1840, Michael in 1842, Johannes in 1844, Joseph in 1846, Franziscus in 1850, Theresia in 1852, Alois in 1854, Mathias in 1858. The first Theresia, born in September 1840, died the same day she was born. The second Theresia, born twelve years later, carried the name forward. Mathias, the youngest, died in 1868 at the age of nine. These losses are recorded without comment in the parish books, as such losses always were, a name and a date and silence.

What kind of life did Michael and Anna Nell lead in those decades between the 1830s and the 1860s? The landscape gives us part of the answer. The volcanic soils of the Sankt Anna am Aigen region supported a mixed agriculture: wine on the south-facing slopes, grain and root crops on the flatter land, pigs and cattle in the farmyards, fruit trees along the roads. It was not the subsistence farming of a mountain community. The wine could be sold. There was a market economy here, connected by the river Raab to the larger world of Feldbach, the district capital fifteen kilometers to the northwest, and from Feldbach to Graz and Vienna beyond. When the Hungarian Western Railway reached Feldbach in 1873, the connection became physical, iron rails replacing the slow rhythm of ox carts on dirt roads.

But before the railway, this was still a world measured by walking distance. A man could walk from Waltra to the church at Sankt Anna in half an hour. To reach Feldbach took most of a day. The great events of the empire, the revolutions of 1848 that abolished the last remnants of serfdom, the wars with Italy and Prussia, the compromise with Hungary in 1867 that created the Dual Monarchy, arrived here as news delivered by travelers, later as dispatches read aloud from the steps of the Tabor in Feldbach. Michael and Anna Nell lived through the abolition of the corvée, the end of the feudal obligations that had bound their parents' generation to the soil. After 1848, they were legally free people, owners of whatever small plot they worked, subject to no lord's demand for unpaid labor. Whether the change made much practical difference in a hamlet of twenty families on a volcanic ridge is another question. The soil was the same. The work was the same. The children still needed feeding.

Waltra — The hamlet within the Sankt Anna am Aigen parish where the Hackl family lived for generations, photographed from the road that still bears the name

Johan and Maria

Michael and Anna Nell's fifth child, Johan Hackl, was born in 1839. He married Maria Gigl, born in 1845, and together they had at least one son who survived to adulthood. Johan died in 1886, at forty-seven. Maria lived until 1919, reaching seventy-four, long enough to see the world she had known dissolve completely: the Habsburg Empire that had governed her entire life collapsed in 1918, the year before her death.

Johan's brother Joseph Hakel, born in 1846, took a different path. He stayed. He married Anna Schuster, born in 1852 in the parish, on the thirtieth of October, 1872, and he died in Sankt Anna am Aigen on the fourteenth of July, 1905, at fifty-nine. His entire life, from birth to burial, played out within the boundaries of the parish where his grandfather Franz had married ninety-four years earlier. His brother Alois, born in 1854, also married locally, wedding Amalia Glanz in January 1883 in the same church. The pattern is clear: most of the children stayed. The parish held them, as the Black Forest had held the Schwehrs and the Würzburgers for generations, by the gravity of land and kinship and the familiar contours of a known world.

But not Johan's son. Johan's son would leave.

Leonhard: From the Village to the City

Johan and Maria's son Leonhard Hackl was born on the sixth of November, 1869, in Hochstraden, the volcanic-ridge hamlet within the Sankt Anna am Aigen parish. His baptismal records give his birthplace with this specificity, placing him not in the market village itself but in the scattered farming settlement on the basalt ridge above it, an eight-kilometer spine of nephelinite running north to south. Hochstraden today has about 145 residents. In 1869, it may have had fewer.

Leonhard's legal domicile, his Zuständigkeit in the language of Habsburg administration, was recorded as Waltra, Bezirk Feldbach. This was a concept without equivalent in the Schwehr country of Baden: your Heimatgemeinde, the municipality responsible for you in matters of poor relief, military service, and legal identity, followed you wherever you went. A man could live in Graz for thirty years and his Zuständigkeit would still be Waltra. It was an administrative thread connecting each subject to a specific place on the map, a bureaucratic version of the gravitational pull that kept most people rooted to the soil. Leonhard would carry Waltra in his documents for the rest of his life, even across the Atlantic.

But sometime in the 1890s, he made a decision that would reshape the family's trajectory. He left the countryside and moved to Graz, the capital of Styria, seventy kilometers to the northwest. It was the kind of move that millions of rural Europeans were making in those decades, the great urbanization that was transforming the continent.

He did not entirely cut his ties. On the eleventh of May, 1896, he returned to Sankt Anna am Aigen to marry Aurelia Maria Küschal. She was twenty-four years old, born on the twenty-first of June, 1871, not in southeastern Styria but in Schöder, a mountain village in the Murau district of Upper Styria, more than a hundred kilometers to the northwest. Her father, Franz Küschal, had been born in 1834 in Schönberg near Knittelfeld, in the industrial heart of Styria. The Küschals were mountain people from the Mur valley, from a colder, higher, more remote part of Styria than the gentle wine country of Sankt Anna.

How Leonhard from the southeastern border and Aurelia from the Upper Styrian mountains found each other is not recorded. Perhaps both had come to Graz independently and met there, as young people from different rural backgrounds did in the anonymity of the city. What we know is that they married in the village church at Sankt Anna am Aigen, that their first child, a daughter also named Aurelia, was born in January 1895, before the wedding, and that after the marriage they returned to Graz to begin their urban life together.

Graz — The Styrian capital where Leonhard Hackl worked as a Dienstmann and raised his family in the working-class Jakomini district

Pestalozzistraße 78

The documents tell us where the Hackl family lived in Graz: Pestalozzistraße 78, in the Jakomini district, the sixth and most densely populated district of the city. Jakomini lay south of the old town, a neighborhood that had grown rapidly in the second half of the nineteenth century as Graz industrialized and workers flooded in from the countryside. It was solidly working-class territory, a place of modest rents and crowded stairwells.

Pestalozzistraße 78, Graz — The building where Friedrich Hackl was born in 1914, in the working-class Jakomini district where the family lived

The parish church that served their neighborhood was St. Josef, a brand-new building in the Florentine Renaissance style that had only been completed in 1908. When Friedrich Hackl was baptized there on the twenty-ninth of January, 1914, the paint on the walls was barely dry.

Leonhard's occupation, as recorded on his son's baptismal certificate, was Dienstmann. The word translates literally as "service man," but in early twentieth-century Graz it meant a porter, a carrier, a man who moved things for other people. Dienstmänner were the human logistics of the pre-automotive city. They met trains at the Hauptbahnhof and carried passengers' luggage. They hauled goods from warehouses to shops. They delivered packages, moved furniture, did whatever physical labor needed doing on any given day. It was honest work, but it was the lowest rung of the urban economy, a job that required nothing but a strong back and a willingness to be on your feet from dawn to dark.

That Leonhard's relative Johanna Hakl, who served as godmother at Friedrich's baptism, was also described as a Dienstmannsgattin, a porter's wife, living at Hamerlinggasse 8, tells us that the Hackl family in Graz formed a small network of relatives all working at the same level of the urban economy. This was typical. Rural migrants to the cities clustered together, sharing information about jobs and housing, creating a village within the city that replicated some of the mutual aid they had known in the countryside.

Graz in the early 1900s was Austria's second-largest city, with a population approaching 150,000. It was a university city, a garrison city, a center of administration for Styria. But it was also a city of stark contrasts. The eastern districts housed the wealthy in comfortable villas near the university. The western and southern quarters were working-class territory: factories and warehouses, craftsmen and immigrants, dense apartment blocks. In the second half of the nineteenth century alone, the city had erected 1,800 new buildings to house the influx of workers. If you lived on Pestalozzistraße in Jakomini, you were working people. You were not poor enough to starve, but you were one bad month away from trouble.

Twelve Children

Leonhard and Aurelia produced twelve children between 1895 and 1914, a span of nineteen years that encompassed the last confident decades of the Habsburg Empire and the moment of its destruction. The first, a daughter also named Aurelia, arrived before the parents' marriage. The last, the twins Friedrich and Margaretha, were born six months before the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand.

Four of the twelve did not survive to adulthood. Maria, born in 1900, died at eight. The eldest daughter Aurelia died in March 1918, at twenty-three, as the war ground toward its end. Franz, born in 1902, died in August 1919, at sixteen, squarely in the period of the Spanish influenza's later waves and the desperate hunger that gripped Austria in the war's aftermath. Friedrich's twin Margaretha, the baby called Gretel, died in infancy. These deaths bracket the war and the pandemic, and they tell us that the Hackl family in Graz was living on the edge, that urban life offered wages but not safety.

The children who survived, Anna, Ella, Adolf, Anton, Josef, Johann (called Hans), and Friedrich, would face the question that the war's aftermath forced on millions: stay or go. Friedrich's baptismal certificate, obtained from the Diocesan Archive of Graz-Seckau by Dr. Alois Ruhri in 2006, preserves the details of his entry into the world: born at nine o'clock in the evening on the twenty-eighth of January, 1914, at Pestalozzistraße 78, baptized the next afternoon by Father Bonaventura von Hellrigl, midwife Anna Lobnig in attendance.

The War and the Collapse

The First World War destroyed Austria. Not just the army, not just the economy, but the entire political and social order that had governed Central Europe for centuries. The Habsburg Empire simply dissolved, like a building whose foundations had been eaten away, and one day the walls fell and there was nothing left but rubble.

For a working-class family in Graz, the war meant hunger. Austria's food supply depended on imports from Hungary, and as the war dragged on and the Allied blockade tightened, those imports dwindled. By 1917, bread rations fell below subsistence levels. People ate turnips, beets, anything they could find. Children suffered most. Diseases that had been declining for decades, tuberculosis, typhus, influenza, roared back as weakened bodies could no longer fight them off.

Then came the armistice in November 1918, and with it not peace but chaos. The empire broke apart. German-speaking Austria, shorn of its domains, was a small, landlocked country of six and a half million people. The economy collapsed. The currency lost its value. And the Spanish influenza struck, killing thousands who had survived four years of war only to die in the pandemic that followed.

Leonhard Hackl was forty-five when the war began and forty-nine when it ended. His daughter Aurelia died in March 1918. His son Franz died in August 1919. The family was being whittled down by the same forces that were destroying the country around them.

It was in this context, this landscape of defeat and hunger and loss, that the idea of emigration took hold.

Jakominiplatz, Graz — The heart of the Jakomini district where the Hackl family lived, in a historical photograph from the early twentieth century

The Decision

America was closing its doors. The quota laws of the early 1920s had slashed the number of immigrants the United States would accept, shutting off the great safety valve that had absorbed Europe's surplus population for a century. But Brazil was opening its doors wider. The abolition of slavery in 1888 had created enormous demand for agricultural labor, and the Brazilian government was actively recruiting European immigrants. Agents circulated through Austrian and German towns, distributing pamphlets that promised free land, fertile soil, and opportunity. For a family with nothing to lose, the offer was irresistible.

We do not know exactly when Leonhard made the decision, or what debates took place in the apartment at Pestalozzistraße. But sometime in late 1924 or early 1925, the preparations were made. The Austrian passport was obtained, number 4751, bearing Leonhard's name and listing his wife Aurelia and their children. They packed whatever they could carry. They said goodbye to Ella, who had married and would stay in Austria. They said goodbye to whatever remained of their life in Graz, the streets Leonhard had walked as a porter, the church where the children had been baptized, the apartment where twelve children had been born and four had died.

And they traveled north, to Amsterdam.

The passport itself, preserved in the family's records, is a poignant document. On the left page, a printed notice headed BITTE! (Please!) asks emigrants to stay in touch with Austria, to report on conditions in their destination country, to maintain contact with their homeland. It speaks of the Austrian government's interest in its overseas citizens, its desire to know about "the economic, climatic, and other conditions" of the places where they settled. The appeal has the tone of a parent watching a child leave home: stay in touch, write to us, don't forget where you came from. On the right page, the immigration stamp from Santos, Brazil, is stamped over the Austrian text, one country's claim literally overwriting another's.

The Orania

The ship was called the Orania, a steamship of the Koninklijke Hollandsche Lloyd, built in Belfast in 1922 and registered in Amsterdam. Her route ran from Amsterdam south through the English Channel, calling at Southampton and Cherbourg, then to Vigo and Lisbon, across to Las Palmas in the Canary Islands, and then the long ocean crossing to Pernambuco, Bahia, Rio de Janeiro, Santos, Montevideo, and Buenos Aires.

The SS Orania — The Dutch steamship that carried the Hackl family from Amsterdam to Santos in February 1925

The Brazilian National Archives in Rio de Janeiro preserve the passenger record. It lists Leonhard Hackl, age fifty-five, profession Agricultor, nationality Austriaco, traveling in third class. His companions: Karolin Hackl, fifty-three (Aurelia's name rendered as the scribes heard it), Anna Hackl, twenty-seven, Johann Hackl, fifteen, and Friedrich Hackl, eleven. Anton, Josef, and Adolf traveled separately or on the same vessel under different entries.

On the tenth of February, 1925, the Orania entered the harbor at Santos. Friedrich Hackl, who had known nothing but the cobblestones and cold rain of Graz, stepped onto a quay where the air was thick with tropical heat and the smell of coffee and diesel fuel and rotting fruit. The harbor was a chaos of languages, Portuguese, Italian, Japanese, German, all the tongues of the immigrant tide that was pouring into Brazil. Stevedores moved bales of coffee toward the warehouses. Porters, men not unlike his father, carrying other people's loads, shouted at one another in a language the boy could not parse into words. The sun was a physical weight. The light was different, flatter and more intense than anything Alpine, and everything was green in a way that the grey streets of Jakomini had never been.

From Santos, the immigrants were sent to the Hospedaria dos Imigrantes in São Paulo, the vast processing center through which every newcomer to the state passed. Medical inspection: eyes, lungs, skin; anyone with a contagious disease faced deportation. Registration: name, age, occupation, destination. Temporary housing in collective dormitories. And then assignment. The Hackls were directed toward the São Paulo interior, to the region around Getulina, where they would begin their Brazilian life on the plantations.

The Hospedaria dos Imigrantes in São Paulo — The immigrant processing center through which every newcomer passed, now the Immigration Museum

The New World

Nothing in Leonhard Hackl's experience had prepared him for Brazil. The interior of São Paulo state in the 1920s was frontier country, raw and green and vast. The Atlantic forest, thick and impenetrable, was being cleared by hand and fire to make way for coffee plantations. The climate was subtropical: hot, humid, with rains that turned the red earth to mud and a sun that burned skin accustomed to Alpine cloud cover. Diseases unknown in Europe, malaria, yellow fever, leishmaniasis, waited in the forest and the standing water. The language was Portuguese, which nobody in the family spoke.

But there was land. There was food. There was a future, or the possibility of one, for the children. In Austria, Leonhard had been a Dienstmann, a porter. In Brazil, he could work toward owning land, toward feeding his family from his own soil. The trade was terrible, leaving everything familiar for everything unknown, but the calculus was clear. Stay in Austria and remain a porter forever. Go to Brazil and become a farmer.

The family eventually made their way from the São Paulo interior to the state of Paraná, to the region that would become the municipality of Mandaguaçu. The town was not officially founded until 1938, but settlers had been arriving since the 1920s. The work was brutal and elemental: a man with an axe and a machete, standing at the edge of forest so dense that the canopy blocked the sky, and cutting. Tree by tree, vine by vine, root by root, carving a clearing out of the green wall. Then burning what remained, the smoke rising in columns visible for miles. Then planting the coffee seedlings in the ashy red earth and waiting three years for the first harvest, surviving on manioc and beans and whatever the half-cleared land would yield in the meantime. The Hackls were among the pioneers. They built a life in a place where everything had to be made from scratch.

Mandaguaçu, Paraná — The pioneer settlement in the Brazilian interior where the Hackl family built their new life

Josef Hackl settled in nearby Altônia, where he would die in 1975. Anton lived until 1984. Friedrich, the eleven-year-old boy who had arrived on the Orania, would eventually move to São Paulo, where he would meet and marry Elisabeth Würzburger in 1955, joining the Hackl line at last with the Black Forest line that had arrived in Brazil by a different route and at a different time.

And then there was Adolf. Adolf Hackl, twenty-four years old when the family arrived, is listed on the Orania's manifest. He entered Brazil with his parents and brothers. And then, at some point that no one in the family can precisely date, he left. He returned to Austria. He could not adjust, or he missed home too much, or he had a reason to return that has been lost. He sailed back across the Atlantic and disappeared into the Austria of the interwar period, a country lurching from crisis to crisis, from the civil war of 1934 to the Anschluss of 1938 to the catastrophe of the Second World War. What became of him, whether he survived, whether he lived to old age in some Austrian village or perished in the violence that consumed the continent, no one knows. He is a gap in the record, a question mark in the family tree.

Those Who Stayed

But the Hackl name never left Sankt Anna am Aigen. While Leonhard carried his family across the Atlantic, the descendants of his uncle Joseph, of his uncle Alois, of the other siblings who had never left the volcanic ridge, remained. They continued to farm the same land, tend the same vineyards, worship in the same parish church. Generation followed generation in the quiet rhythm that had governed these hamlets since Franz Hakel married Zäzilia Waltersdorfer in 1811.

Today, two centuries later, the name persists in the same places. A Franz Hackl runs a Buschenschank, a traditional wine tavern, at Sichauf 21, serving home-cured meats from his own pigs and wines from the same volcanic slopes. A Robert Hackl operates an agricultural services business at Waltra 2, the very hamlet where Franz and Zäzilia's children were born in the 1820s. A Dietmar Franz Hackl runs an electrical workshop from the same address. And Stefan Hackl chairs the local football club, the USV St. Anna am Aigen. The Buschenschank uses the old spelling, Hakl, the same form that appears on Leonhard's baptismal certificate, as though the orthographic thread connecting the parish register of 1869 to the wine label of 2025 had never been cut.

Whether these families descend from Joseph, from Alois, from one of the other siblings who stayed, we cannot say with certainty. But the concentration of the name in the same hamlets, Waltra, Sichauf, the same ridge above the same church, across two hundred years, speaks for itself. The Hackls who left built new lives in Paraná and São Paulo. The Hackls who stayed kept the old life going, pressing wine from grapes grown on the same basalt, baking bread in the same kind of ovens their great-great-grandparents had used. The two branches have not been in contact. But the soil that connects them is the same.

Leonhard's End

Leonhard Hackl died on the eighth of July, 1953, in Maringá, Paraná, Brazil. He was eighty-three years old. He had been born in a hamlet of 145 souls on a volcanic ridge in southeastern Styria, baptized in the parish church of Sankt Anna am Aigen, raised among vineyards that grew on two-million-year-old basalt. He had left that world for the city, had worked the streets of Graz as a porter, had raised twelve children in a cramped apartment in Jakomini while the Habsburg Empire crumbled around him. He had survived a world war, a pandemic, an economic collapse, and the death of four of his children. At the age of fifty-five, he had boarded a steamship in Amsterdam and crossed the Atlantic with his family, arriving in a tropical country where he knew no one, spoke nothing of the language, and understood nothing of the climate or the diseases or the soil. And he had made it work. He had cleared land, planted crops, built a home, raised the children who survived to adulthood, and lived long enough to see grandchildren born on Brazilian soil who would never know the volcanic hills of Hochstraden or the cobblestones of Pestalozzistraße.

His wife Aurelia survived him by nine years, dying on the thirtieth of July, 1962, also in Maringá, at the age of ninety-one. She had been born in the mountains of Upper Styria, had married in the wine country of the southeast, had raised her family in the city, and had ended her days in the red earth of Paraná. Ninety-one years, two continents, one language slowly giving way to another as the grandchildren grew up speaking Portuguese and the old German words faded from daily use.

Leonhard was buried in Brazil, far from the churchyard at Sankt Anna am Aigen where his father Johan lay, and where his grandfather Michael lay before him, and where the bones of Franz Hakel, the earliest name in the line, had returned to the volcanic earth sometime in the first half of the nineteenth century. The distance between those graves, one in the basalt soil of Styria and the other in the red laterite of Paraná, measures exactly the distance this family traveled in four generations: from village to city to continent, from vineyard to apartment block to coffee plantation, from the old world to the new.

And in São Paulo, a young man named Friedrich, who had been called Fritz in Graz and was now learning to answer to Frederico, was about to meet a woman named Elisabeth Würzburger, born in Ohrensbach in the Glottertal, whose family's story we have already told. That meeting, and the union of the two lines, is the final chapter of this history.
