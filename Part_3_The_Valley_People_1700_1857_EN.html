Part Three: The Valley People
Heuweiler, Glottertal, and the Roots of Departure
1700–1857
By the year 1700, the family that had once been the von Brauns of Ottobeuren had become something else entirely. They were now the Schwehrs and the Schwärs, monastery people and mountain craftsmen, scattered across the high ridges and foothill valleys between Sankt Peter and the Rhine plain. The "von" was gone. The noble status that had once set them apart in the Swabian lowlands had dissolved into the democratic poverty of the Black Forest, where everyone worked with their hands and no one stood very far above anyone else. But if they had lost their title, they had gained something perhaps more valuable: a place. And the place was Heuweiler.

Heuweiler — A historical view of the village in the foothills of the Black Forest that became the Schwehr family's home for more than two centuries

Bartholomäus Descends

The pivot point between the high forest and the valley came in the person of Bartholomäus Schwehr, born in 1679 in Sankt Peter. The spelling of his surname had shifted: what had been Schwär in his father Christianus's time was now recorded as Schwehr, a change that probably reflects nothing more than a different parish clerk in a different village writing down the same Alemannic sounds. Bartholomäus was one of thirteen children, and like younger sons throughout history, he had to find his own path. The monastery could not absorb them all.

Sometime in the early 1700s, Bartholomäus left Sankt Peter and moved northwest, descending from the high ridge into the gentler country where the Black Forest gave way to the Rhine plain. In the spring of 1717, he married Anna Ihringer in Heuweiler, a village he would make his permanent home. Her surname suggests roots in the nearby wine-growing town of Ihringen on the Kaiserstuhl, that volcanic hill rising from the Rhine flatlands west of Freiburg. The marriage of a mountain man from Sankt Peter and a woman possibly connected to the warmer, more prosperous wine country was a meeting of two different worlds within the same small region.

Heuweiler in 1717 was a village of perhaps three hundred souls, strung along a shallow valley where a small stream flowed west toward the larger Glotter and eventually into the Elz and the Rhine. It was neither a mountain settlement nor a lowland town but something in between, perched in the foothills at about three hundred meters, high enough to see the Black Forest ridges to the east but low enough to grow grain and even a little wine on the south-facing slopes. The village belonged to the Amt Waldkirch, under the authority of the Margrave of Baden, and its church served a Catholic parish that had endured since the Middle Ages.

Among Bartholomäus and Anna's six children was Joannes Baptista Schwehr, born in the late summer of 1730 and baptized the same day in the parish church. Bartholomäus himself died in 1736, when the boy was only six. He had accomplished what he set out to do: he had established his family in a new place, in a village where they would remain for generations.

The parish church at Heuweiler — Where the Schwehr family baptized their children and buried their dead for more than two centuries

The Gschwander Marriage

In January 1754, Joannes Baptista Schwehr married Maria Gschwander in the Heuweiler parish church. He was twenty-three. She was seventeen, from Glottertal, the long valley just to the south.

The Gschwander family was Swiss-Alemannic in origin, part of that constant trickle of families that moved north from the Swiss cantons into the German Black Forest over centuries. Maria's father, Martin Gschwander, had married Maria Schurhammer in 1728 at the church in Unterglottertal. Both surnames point to Swiss or Alemannic roots. They had at least nine children, scattering them across the valley and its surrounding villages.

Joannes Baptista and Maria settled in Heuweiler and raised nine children. But neither parent lived to see them all grown. Joannes Baptista died in June 1772, at forty-one. Maria survived him by seven years, dying in 1779 at forty-two. Both deaths left their youngest son, Georg Schwehr, born in May 1770, an orphan before he reached the age of nine.

The early eighteenth century brought transformation to the political geography of southwestern Germany. The old mosaic of tiny lordships, free cities, and ecclesiastical states was being consolidated into larger territorial units. Meanwhile, just across the valley, the Glottertal villages belonged not to Baden at all but to Vorderösterreich, Further Austria, the scattered Habsburg possessions in southwestern Germany. From 1567 until the Napoleonic wars, the people of the Glottertal were subjects of the Habsburg Emperor in Vienna, not the Margrave in Karlsruhe. A man from Heuweiler marrying a woman from Glottertal was, in a technical sense, an international marriage. Yet the villages were only a few kilometers apart, spoke the same dialect, and attended the same Catholic churches.

The Valley of the Glotter

Glottertal, the Valley of the Glotter, stretches about eight kilometers eastward from the edge of the Rhine plain into the Black Forest, following the course of the Glotter stream from its mouth near Denzlingen up to the summit of the Kandel at 1,241 meters. In that short distance, the valley encompasses almost a thousand meters of vertical relief, from sun-warmed vineyards at its western opening to dark spruce forests and bare mountain meadows at its eastern head.

The valley's history reaches back to the Romans, who mined silver, zinc, and iron in these mountains. Old tunnel entrances and spoil heaps still mark the hillsides. The first written mention of the Glottertal settlements appears in 1112, in the chronicles of the Abbey of Sankt Peter. A parish church existed by 1275, and in 1469 a confraternity of the Virgin Mary was founded by the combined efforts of all four village communities — Unterglottertal, Oberglottertal, Ohrensbach, and Föhrental — a rare display of cooperation among settlements that otherwise jealously guarded their independence. These four communities remained separate municipalities until 1970, each with its own character, its own families, its own relationship to the land.

It was in Föhrental, the most remote of these hamlets, that the Würzburger family put down roots so deep that they have never been pulled up.

The Würzburger of Föhrental

The Würzburger name is inseparable from the Glottertal. It has been there so long and is still so present that the family seems almost to have grown out of the soil itself, like the grapevines on the south-facing slopes or the dark firs on the mountain flanks above. To tell the story of the Würzburgers is to tell the story of the valley.

The earliest Würzburger we can trace is Simon Würzburger, born in 1714, who married Katharina Scherzinger around 1730. The surname itself is a riddle. Würzburg is a city in Franconia, northern Bavaria, hundreds of kilometers from the Black Forest. Did some distant ancestor come from there? Was the name given to a migrant family by their new neighbors, as was common practice, the "people from Würzburg" becoming simply "the Würzburgers"? We cannot know. But by the 1730s, the family was established in Föhrental, and there they would remain.

Simon died young, at only thirty-five. But his son Georg Würzburger, born in 1740 in Föhrental, survived to build the family's presence in the valley. He married Maria Strecker in 1759, and together they had twelve children. The Würzburger line continued through their son Joseph, born in 1764, who lived to seventy-three and married twice. His second wife, Maria Raufer, bore him seven children, among them Barbara Würzburger, born in May 1829 at the Würzburgerhof, the family's named farmstead in Föhrental.

A named farm. This detail, preserved in the parish Familienblätter, tells us something important. In the Black Forest tradition, farms were given names that persisted across generations regardless of who owned them. The Würzburgerhof was not just a piece of land; it was an identity, a place with a history of its own. The fact that the farm bore the family surname suggests that the Würzburgers had been there long enough for the place and the people to have become the same thing.

The Familienblätter record Barbara's occupation as Taglöhnerin, a day laborer, the lowest rung of the rural economy. Life was hard in Föhrental. The growing season was short, the soil thin, the farms small. Barbara married Hermann Blattmann, and their son Kasimir Würzburger, born in January 1858, climbed a step higher. The records list his occupation as Schreiner, a joiner or cabinetmaker, a skilled trade that required years of training. From day laborer to craftsman in one generation: this was the trajectory of ambition in a world where progress was measured not in fortunes but in the acquisition of skill.

Kasimir married Rosa Beck in 1890, and they had a son, Josef Würzburger, born in February 1893 in Unterglottertal. Josef too became a Schreiner, inheriting his father's craft — and inheriting, perhaps without knowing it, something of the woodworking tradition that had entered the family centuries earlier through the Ruff craftsmen of Gütenbach. He would marry a woman named Anna Luise Gehri in 1919, and in 1933, at the age of forty, he would board a ship for Brazil, carrying with him the joinery skills of the Black Forest and a surname that would persist in the New World just as it had persisted in the old.

The Würzburger family remains deeply woven into the life of the Glottertal to this day. A Heinrich Würzburger, still living in Glottertal, authored the definitive local history of the valley, published on the community website an-der-glotter.de, and served as chairman of the Sängerbund Glottertal, the valley's choral society. A Bernhard Würzburger produced a documentary film about the valley, described in the local press as "a love declaration to the Glottertal." A Josef Würzburger Energie operates a business on the Föhrentalstraße. In the records of the Trachtenkapelle Glottertal, the valley's traditional brass band, a Manuela Würzburger earned a gold music achievement badge for her flute playing. The name appears on mailboxes and doorbells and business directories throughout the valley, as rooted in the Glottertal as the old farmsteads themselves.

Föhrental — The hamlet in the Glottertal where the Würzburger family has lived since at least the 1730s, and where the Würzburgerhof still stands

The Schill Connection and the Age of Hunger

Meanwhile, back in Heuweiler, the orphan Georg Schwehr had grown to manhood and married Maria Schill in the late summer of 1791. The Schill family was deeply rooted in the village. The Schills, the Zimmermanns, the Merzes: these were the old Heuweiler families, their surnames appearing again and again in the parish registers like the recurring motifs of a fugue.

Georg and Maria produced twelve children and lived through two decades of European war, from the French Revolution through Napoleon's reshaping of Germany. The old ecclesiastical principalities were dissolved. Sankt Peter and Sankt Märgen were shuttered. The Glottertal, having been Austrian for nearly 250 years, was suddenly transferred to Baden. Georg died in 1818, three years after Waterloo. Maria survived him by thirty years, dying in 1848, at seventy-seven, in yet another year of revolution.

Their son Franz Joseph Schwehr, born in 1804, married Barbara Maerz in 1824. Barbara was the daughter of Johann Georg Merz and Magdalena Reichenbach, connecting to still more old families. The Reichenbach name, like the Würzburger name in the Glottertal, has survived in the region. A Daniel Reichenbach recently served as commander of the Glottertal volunteer fire department, and Reichenbachs appear in the brass band rosters alongside the Würzburgers, both families still tending the same valley their ancestors settled centuries ago.

Franz Joseph and Barbara had six children. Some died in infancy, as children did with numbing regularity. Among those who survived was Georg Schwehr, born in April 1827, named for his grandfather. This Georg would be the last Schwehr in the direct line before the family name passed, through his daughter Afra, into other surnames and eventually across the ocean.

The Rüd Valley and the Great Departure

While the Schwehrs were in Heuweiler, another lineage was unfolding in the neighboring mountains. In Untersimonswald, deeper in the mountains, lived the Rüd family, whose name would later be spelled Ruth.

Simonswald — The mountain valley where the Rüd/Ruth family lived before moving to Heuweiler

Michael Rüd, born in 1705, married three times, a pattern that tells its own story of mortality. Through his second wife Magdalena Widmann, the line continued to Matthias Rüd, who married Ursula Wehrle in 1780. Their son Jakob Ruth, as the name was now spelled, was born in 1789 in Simonswald. The spelling shift from Rüd to Ruth was not deliberate but the natural drift that occurred when Alemannic dialect names were written down by different scribes over the generations.

Jakob moved to the Heuweiler area and married Maria Schill in 1820. They had ten children, and their family's story illuminates one of the great themes of the century: emigration. Of those ten children, at least four left Germany for the New World.

Joseph Ruth emigrated to Canada and settled in Woolwich, in the Waterloo region of Ontario. Lambert Alexander Ruth went to Missouri. Peter Ruth settled in Bruce County, Ontario. Jakob Ruth the younger followed his brothers to Waterloo. The pattern was unmistakable: one brother left, established himself, sent word back, and another followed.

The Waterloo region they found was itself a landscape shaped by German-speaking settlers. Mennonites from Pennsylvania had arrived first, in the early 1800s, and their presence drew further waves of German immigration. By the 1860s, when the Ruth brothers were establishing themselves, the town of Berlin — later renamed Kitchener during the anti-German fever of the First World War — had German-language newspapers, German churches, German breweries, and streets where you could live your entire life without speaking English. Jakob Ruth the younger married Mary Schill in Waterloo in 1867; the Schill name, carried across the Atlantic, found new ground in Canada alongside the Ruth name that had once been Rüd. The family lines cast by these brothers would linger in the Waterloo region for generations. It is one of those quiet symmetries of history that the same area would draw other members of this extended family, from a different branch and a different century, as if the place itself were a kind of magnet.

But not all the Ruth children left. Theresia Ruth, born in 1832 in Heuweiler, stayed. She was the daughter who would marry into the Schwehr family and produce the generation that concerns us most directly.

German emigration to the Americas in the nineteenth century was one of the largest mass movements in human history. Between 1820 and 1920, approximately six million Germans left for the United States alone, with hundreds of thousands more going to Canada, Brazil, and Argentina. The Black Forest was one of the regions most heavily affected. Poverty, land fragmentation, political repression, and failed revolutions drove people out. What made the Ruth family's story unusual was not the leaving but the staying: Theresia's decision to remain in Heuweiler and marry a local man kept one thread of the family anchored to the soil their ancestors had worked for generations.

Georg Schwehr and Theresia Ruth

The central marriage of this chapter took place in June 1858. The groom was Georg Schwehr, son of Franz Joseph and Barbara Maerz, born in 1827. The bride was Theresia Ruth, daughter of Jakob and Maria Schill, born in 1832. Both were from Heuweiler, both Catholic, both from families that had worked the same land for generations.

They settled not in Heuweiler but in Gundelfingen, closer to Freiburg. The move was driven by the same pressures that had sent Theresia's brothers across the Atlantic: there was simply not enough land. They produced twelve children. Among the first was Afra Schwehr, born on the twentieth of August, 1857, in Gundelfingen, before her parents' formal marriage the following June. Such sequences were not unusual when betrothal, rather than the church ceremony, was considered the real beginning of married life.

Afra. The name is unusual, even striking. Saint Afra was a martyr of the early Church, venerated particularly in Augsburg, where she was said to have been burned alive during the persecution of Diocletian in 304. Her cult was strong in the old Swabian lands. The choice of name may reflect a family memory, conscious or not, of the ancient connection to Swabia and Ottobeuren, that distant starting point five hundred years and two hundred kilometers to the east. Or it may simply have been the name of a godmother. We cannot know. But the name stands out in the parish register among the Marias and Barbaras and Theresias like a single bright thread in a somber tapestry.

Georg Schwehr did not live to see most of his children grow. He died in April 1866, in Wildtal, at thirty-eight. The cause of death is not recorded in the sources available to us, but thirty-eight was not an unusual age to die in a world where tuberculosis was endemic, where manual labor wore bodies down early, and where a man with twelve children and insufficient land was unlikely to have eaten well or rested much. Wildtal was a hamlet near Gundelfingen where the family had moved, perhaps seeking cheaper housing closer to day-labor work. Theresia carried on for eleven more years, dying in 1877, at forty-five. When she died, Afra was twenty.

What was Afra's life in those years? She was the eldest daughter of a man who had died when she was eight and a mother who would die when she was twenty. She would have taken on responsibilities early — caring for younger siblings, working for wages, learning the domestic economy that was a woman's survival skill in a household without a father's income. By the time Theresia died, Afra was already on the threshold of her own life, shaped by loss and by the fierce self-reliance that loss demanded. The Schwehr children scattered after their mother's death. Martin and Fridolin each married Gehri sisters from Wildtal. Georg the younger emigrated to Canada and married Mary Meyer in Waterloo, Ontario, following the path his Ruth uncles had blazed. And Afra herself married Georg Gehri, from the same Wildtal family that had absorbed her brothers.

The Meaning of the Valley

For 157 years, from Bartholomäus's arrival around 1717 to Afra's birth in 1857, Heuweiler had been the center of the Schwehr family's world. Seven generations were born there, baptized in the same church, married at the same altar, buried in the same churchyard. The neighboring Glottertal held the Würzburgers and the Reichenbachs with equal tenacity, their roots reaching down through the thin mountain soil into something deeper than any single generation could have planted.

It was a small world. A man could walk the entire length of Heuweiler in twenty minutes. Everyone knew everyone. The same surnames appeared in every generation. The parish priest knew every family by name, recorded each birth and death in the same register, adding each new entry to a book that already held the names of grandparents and great-grandparents.

But the valley was also changing. The population was growing. Land was shrinking. The Ruth brothers had shown the way out: across the ocean. Georg Schwehr the younger had followed them to Canada. Others went to Brazil, Argentina, Australia. The great hemorrhage of people from the German countryside was transforming these villages from self-contained worlds into way stations, places people came from rather than places they stayed.

Afra Schwehr, born in 1857, was a child of this transition. She carried in her name the memory of an ancient Swabian saint. In her blood she carried the inheritance of a dozen family lines: Schwehr and Schill and Merz and Zimmermann and Gschwander and Rüd and Wehrle and Widmann, monastery people and craftsmen, mountain farmers and valley dwellers. She would marry Georg Gehri and bring into the world a daughter named Anna Luise, born in 1889 in Wildtal. And Anna Luise would marry Josef Würzburger, the Schreiner from Föhrental, uniting in one marriage two family lines that had lived within a few kilometers of each other for two centuries but had never before been joined. Together they would make the decision that none of their ancestors had made: to leave not just a village but a continent.

The land had shaped them. It had made them patient, because mountain farming requires patience. It had made them resourceful, because the soil was poor and you had to find other ways to live. It had made them stubborn, because the winters were long and only the stubborn survived. And it had made them ready, when the time came, to leave.

The valley had held them for a century and a half. It would not hold them forever.
