Part One: The Fortress
Ottobeuren and the von Braun Nobility
1352–1450
In the year of our Lord 1352, when the wounds of the great pestilence were still fresh across Christendom and the Holy Roman Empire struggled to comprehend the catastrophe that had claimed perhaps half its subjects, a child was born in the Benedictine town of Ottobeuren in the Swabian lands. His name was Hans Peter Adam, and he bore the surname Braun with the particle "von" before it, marking him as a man of noble birth in an age when such distinctions meant the difference between holding land and working it, between commanding labor and performing it, between eating meat on feast days and subsisting on rye bread and thin beer. This is where our story begins, not with the grand migrations that would come later, not with the Black Forest settlements or the Brazilian wilderness, but here in this monastery town where church bells marked the hours and the great abbey ruled every soul within its reach.

The Benedictine World

To understand Hans Peter Adam Braun and his family, one must first understand Ottobeuren itself. The abbey had stood in this valley of the Unterallgäu for nearly six hundred years by the time Hans Peter was born, founded in the year 764 by an Alemannic nobleman named Silach in the reign of the Frankish kings. It was old when Charlemagne was crowned emperor. It had weathered the Magyar raids, the investiture controversy, the rise and fall of dynasties. In 972, the Emperor Otto the Great himself had granted the abbey its great charter of freedom, the Grosser Freiheitsbrief, which freed it from all imperial taxes and services and guaranteed the monks the right to elect their own abbot without interference from king or duke or bishop.

This imperial immediacy, this direct subjection to the emperor alone with no lord standing between, was no small thing. It meant that Ottobeuren was not merely a monastery but a principality, a small ecclesiastical state within the Holy Roman Empire. The abbot governed villages and farms, mills and forests, administered justice, and collected rents and tithes. By the fourteenth century, the abbey's territories encompassed twenty-seven villages scattered across more than two hundred and fifty square kilometers of rolling Swabian countryside, and some ten thousand souls answered to the abbot's authority in matters both sacred and profane.

The town of Ottobeuren itself clung to the abbey's walls like a child to its mother's skirts. The great church, rebuilt in Romanesque grandeur in the twelfth century, rose above the thatched roofs and narrow lanes. Its bells called the faithful to prayer at dawn, at midday, at vespers, at compline. The monks chanted the Divine Office in Latin according to the Rule of Saint Benedict, that ancient prescription for ordered communal life written in the sixth century: Ora et labora, pray and work. Seven times daily the monks gathered in their choir stalls to sing the psalms. Between these sacred hours, they copied manuscripts in the scriptorium, tended the sick in the infirmary, managed the abbey's vast agricultural operations, administered justice in the abbot's court, collected revenues, supervised construction, and trained the sons of nobles in letters and law.

The Benedictine monastery at Ottobeuren was part of a vast network of such institutions that formed the intellectual and administrative backbone of medieval Europe. In the twelfth century, under the blessed Abbot Rupert I, Ottobeuren became a center of the Hirsau reform movement, which sought to restore strict observance of the Benedictine Rule and to free monasteries from excessive secular interference. The abbey's scriptorium produced illuminated manuscripts that survive to this day in German libraries. By the fourteenth century, however, monastic discipline had begun to slip. The abbey was wealthy, perhaps too wealthy, and it was run increasingly as an economic enterprise rather than a house of prayer. In 1356, it came under the jurisdiction of the Bishop of Augsburg, losing some of its cherished independence. By 1471, spiritual decay would reach such a nadir that only two monks remained in residence. But in 1352, when Hans Peter Adam was born, such decline was still a century away.

The Black Death

Hans Peter Adam's birth came only two years after the great dying, the pestilence that contemporary chroniclers called simply die grosse Sterblichkeit, the great mortality. To understand what the Braun family had survived, one must understand what that pestilence did to the world they inhabited.

The pestilence entered Europe through Italian ports in 1347 and reached the German lands by 1349. The chronicles tell us it arrived in Augsburg and Ulm in the summer and autumn of that year, spreading east from Strasbourg toward the great imperial cities of Swabia. Ottobeuren, lying between Augsburg and Lake Constance along the old Roman roads, cannot have escaped.

The disease manifested in several forms, all of them terrible. The bubonic variety produced grotesque swellings in the groin and armpits, the lymph nodes turning black with internal hemorrhage — hence the name that would later be given to the pestilence: the Black Death. Victims burned with fever and often died within three to five days. The pneumonic form attacked the lungs, spread through the air, and killed even more quickly, sometimes within twenty-four hours.

No one understood what caused it. Learned physicians blamed a conjunction of planets, a corruption of the air. Some said it was God's punishment for human sin. Others blamed the Jews, accusing them of poisoning wells, and terrible pogroms swept through the Rhineland and Swabia in 1349 and 1350. Across the region, Jewish communities that had existed for centuries were annihilated in a matter of weeks. The violence reached the countryside around Ottobeuren: the imperial cities of Augsburg and Ulm expelled or killed their Jewish populations, and the abbey lands between them were no island of tolerance. The disease, of course, paid no attention to such scapegoating. It killed rich and poor, noble and peasant, Christian and Jew alike.

Modern scholarship estimates that the Black Death killed between 30 and 60 percent of Europe's population between 1347 and 1353. The total population of the continent fell from perhaps 75 million to as few as 50 million. It would take more than a century and a half for population levels to recover. The social and economic consequences were profound and permanent. The old feudal order could not survive the catastrophic labor shortage that followed. Serfdom began its long decline. Wages rose sharply. Workers gained mobility and bargaining power. The rigid hierarchies of medieval society loosened, never to be fully restored. The fourteenth century had already been marked by crisis before the plague arrived: the warm, stable weather of the High Middle Ages had given way to what scholars now call the Little Ice Age, and the Great Famine of 1315–16 had killed perhaps one in ten across northern Europe. The plague fell upon a population already weakened and afraid.

A Family That Survived

And yet the Braun family survived. Hans Peter Adam Braun was born on the seventh day of November in 1352, in the town of Reimlingen in the Ries basin north of Ottobeuren. His father's name is lost to us, but we know his mother was Maria Catharina Stiff, born in 1357 in Ottobeuren itself. The Stiff family was local, rooted in the abbey's lands, part of that class of free peasants and minor gentry who formed the backbone of rural society.

Why did they survive when so many others did not? No record tells us directly, but the circumstances suggest some possibilities. Ottobeuren was not a crossroads of trade but a monastery town set back from the major routes. The plague traveled fastest along roads that carried merchants and soldiers; settlements even a few miles off the main paths sometimes escaped the worst. The abbey, too, had resources that ordinary villages lacked: granaries, livestock, an infirmary staffed by monks who knew something of herbs and care. A family attached to the abbey's lands — fed from its stores, sheltered within its orbit — stood a better chance than tenant farmers exposed to every passing contagion and every failed harvest. Perhaps this is why the Brauns endured. Or perhaps it was simply luck, the same blind fortune that decided which households lived and which were wiped out entirely. We cannot know. But they endured, and that endurance is the foundation of everything that follows.

The couple married on the first of July, 1380, in Reimlingen. Hans Peter was twenty-seven years old, Maria Catharina twenty-three. These were typical ages for marriage in a post-plague world where land had become available and young people could afford to establish households earlier than their parents' generation had managed. The wedding would have taken place at the church door, as was customary, with the priest blessing their union and the couple exchanging vows in the vernacular German before witnesses from both families.

They made their home in Ottobeuren. Hans Peter bore the designation "von Braun," which marked him as noble, but what did nobility mean in such a time and place? It did not mean vast estates or coats of arms emblazoned on castle walls. It meant, perhaps, exemption from certain taxes and labor services. It meant the right to bear arms and to own a war horse. It meant precedence in church and at table. It meant, most practically, the holding of land not as a tenant but as an inheritor, with the right to pass that land to one's children.

In 1380, at the time of Hans Peter's marriage, their first child was already born or soon would be. Christian Braun came into the world that year, the first of what would be a single surviving line. In a time of high infant mortality, when perhaps half of all children died before reaching adulthood, the survival of any child to maturity was a victory.

Life in a Monastery Town

As a family of minor nobility, the Brauns occupied a particular niche in Ottobeuren's hierarchy. They held land from the abbot — perhaps a few dozen acres of fields, meadow, and woodland — and owed rents in coin or kind, labor services at harvest time, and military service if the abbot needed armed men. In exchange, they had security of tenure and the right to pass their holding to their children. It was the abbey that gave this arrangement its shape. The monks owned the mills where every household ground its grain and the ovens where bread was baked, and every family paid its dues for these services. The abbot's court settled disputes over boundaries, inheritance, debts, and the small injuries of village life. No one in Ottobeuren could be born, marry, farm, trade, or die without the abbey's involvement.

The agricultural year governed everything else. In spring, the great wooden plow cut furrows in heavy clay soil. Rye and oats were the main crops in this part of Swabia, better suited to the cool, damp climate than wheat or barley. Through summer, the fields needed weeding, the meadows mowing for hay. In August and September came the harvest, that desperate race against weather and time when every able body was needed. The grain had to be cut, sheaved, threshed, and winnowed before the autumn rains came.

The post-plague economy was characterized by chronic labor shortages and rising wages. Across Europe, governments tried to freeze wages at pre-plague levels and restrict workers' mobility, but these laws were widely ignored. By 1400, most agricultural labor in southern Germany was performed by free workers hired for wages or by tenant farmers who paid rent in coin rather than labor. This shift gave peasants more autonomy but also more insecurity. The chroniclers of the time noted with disapproval that peasants were wearing better clothes, eating meat more often, and showing less deference to their betters. The old order was dying, though few yet recognized it.

The Next Generation

Christian Braun, born in 1380, grew to manhood in this changing world. Around 1399, at the age of nineteen, he married Eva Catherina, a woman whose surname has not come down to us but who was born in 1382 in Ottobeuren. Like his father, Christian married a local woman, keeping the family rooted in the abbey lands.

Their son Johann Bernhard Braun was born in the year 1400, at the very turning of the century. His birth came as the old century ended and the new began, though such calendrical milestones meant little to most people, who reckoned time by feast days and harvests, by the reigns of emperors and popes, by the cycles of birth and death in their own families.

Johann Bernhard's childhood would have been shaped by the rhythms of the church year. On Sundays and feast days, work ceased and the family attended Mass at the abbey church. They would have stood in the nave with the other laypeople, the great rood screen separating them from the choir where the monks chanted their Latin offices. The priest elevated the Host and the bell rang and everyone knelt. Most could not understand the Latin words, but they knew the gestures, the standing and kneeling and crossing, the ritual that connected them to God and to their ancestors who had performed these same motions for generations.

Johann Bernhard would have been baptized within days of his birth, the ceremony performed at the abbey church with a priest pouring holy water over his head while godparents spoke vows on his behalf. If he survived the dangerous first year of life, he would have been raised partly by his mother and partly by the community of children who played in the lanes and alleys of Ottobeuren. By age seven or so, he would have begun learning his father's work, following Christian into the fields, watching how to plow and sow and reap, how to present oneself before the abbot's court, how to behave as a man of his station.

Johann Bernhard's Marriage

Around 1425, at the age of twenty-five, Johann Bernhard Braun married Eva Catherina Bauer. She was nineteen years old, born in 1406 in Ottobeuren to Christian Bauer and his wife. The Bauer name meant "farmer," though many families bore occupational surnames that no longer reflected their actual work. Christian Bauer had lived from 1382 to 1434, and his daughter's marriage to a Braun, a family with the "von" distinction, suggests the Bauers had some standing in the community.

The marriage was almost certainly arranged by the fathers, as was customary. Young people of this class did not marry for romantic love, though affection might grow over time. They married to consolidate land, to forge alliances between families, to produce heirs who would carry on the family name and inherit its property. The wedding contract would have specified what each party brought to the marriage: land, livestock, household goods, coin. These arrangements were serious business, often negotiated over months and recorded in detail to prevent future disputes.

Johann Bernhard and Eva Catherina established their household in Ottobeuren, continuing the family's three-generation residence in the shadow of the abbey. Their first child, Jacob Braun, was born in 1420, possibly before the formal marriage, which was not uncommon when a bride was already pregnant. Five years into the marriage came a momentous year: 1430 brought not one but two sons, or perhaps the records conflate two births that occurred within a year of each other.

Adam Luppert von Braun and Blasius von Braun entered the world in 1430. The presence of the "von" in their names is significant. The family was maintaining its noble status into the fourth generation, passing it down from Hans Peter Adam through Christian to Johann Bernhard and now to his sons. Blasius was named for Saint Blaise, the Armenian bishop martyred in 316, whose feast day fell on February third. Blaise was particularly venerated in the German lands as a patron saint of throat ailments, and on his feast day the priest would bless throats with two crossed candles. The name suggests that the family maintained its Catholic devotions, decades before Martin Luther would split Christendom in two.

The World of 1430

The year of Blasius and Adam's birth came eighty years after the first plague outbreak. Europe had begun to recover, though slowly. Population was creeping back up. Villages that had been abandoned were being resettled. Fields that had gone to scrub were being plowed again. But the plague had not disappeared. It returned in waves throughout the fifteenth century, striking here and there with seemingly random cruelty, carrying off the young and healthy, sparing others for no reason anyone could discern.

In Ottobeuren specifically, the abbey was entering a period of decline that would reach its nadir in 1471, when only two monks remained. The great landholding monasteries of the Middle Ages were struggling in an age of changing economics. The Black Death had disrupted their labor force and their revenue streams. Younger sons of noble families, who in earlier centuries might have become monks and administrators, were now more likely to seek their fortunes in the expanding cities or in the military service of princes and emperors.

Yet for a family like the Brauns, the abbey remained the center of their world. The abbot still held jurisdiction over local disputes. The monastery still owned the mills and the ovens, and every household paid its dues.

The End of an Era

Johann Bernhard's wife, Eva Catherina Bauer, died in 1448 at the age of forty-two. She was buried in the abbey's graveyard, that consecrated ground that had received the bodies of Ottobeuren's dead for seven centuries. No stone marked her grave, or if it did, it has not survived. Such monuments were reserved for abbots and great nobles, not for the wives of minor gentry. But her sons remembered her, and her name has come down to us through the careful records that someone, somewhere, maintained across the centuries.

Two years later, in 1450, Johann Bernhard Braun himself died. He was fifty years old, which was not a bad age for the time. He had survived infancy, the most dangerous time of life. He had survived to adulthood and married and fathered children. He had lived through plague years and harvest failures, through cold winters and wet summers. He had maintained his family's status and passed it on to his sons. In the great ledger of medieval lives, this counted as success.

He left behind three sons: Jacob, now thirty years old; Adam Luppert, twenty; and Blasius, also twenty. The younger two would live long lives, Blasius until 1510 and Adam until 1499, spanning the entire latter half of the fifteenth century. They would witness the end of the Middle Ages and the beginning of the early modern period. They would see the Ottoman Turks conquer Constantinople in 1453, ending the thousand-year Byzantine Empire. Adam would die just seven years after Christopher Columbus reached the Americas, though he might never have heard of that voyage.

The Departure

But more immediately relevant to our story, Johann Bernhard's sons would be the generation that left Ottobeuren. The family that had been rooted there for four generations, that had survived the Black Death and its aftermath, that had maintained its noble status through changing times, would not remain in the abbey's shadow forever.

What drove them to leave? The abbey itself was failing. With only a handful of monks left by the 1460s, the institution that had given the town its purpose and its livelihood was hollowing out. The abbot's court still sat, the mills still ground, but the vitality was draining away. For young men with the "von" before their names, Ottobeuren's established hierarchies offered little room. The positions that mattered were held by older men. The land was divided. The opportunities that had opened after the plague — when empty holdings could be claimed and vacant roles filled — had long since closed again.

To the west lay the Black Forest, that great expanse of mountains and deep valleys stretching from the Rhine to the Swabian plateau. It was being opened to new settlement. Monasteries there needed administrators, villages needed craftsmen, lords needed soldiers and stewards. The journey from Ottobeuren to the northern Black Forest was perhaps two hundred kilometers — a week's travel by wagon over roads that were little more than cart tracks through river valleys and over low passes. It was not a journey undertaken lightly. To leave meant to abandon the graves of parents and grandparents, to sever the ties of community and obligation that had held the family in place for a century, to gamble everything on the promise of better prospects in an unfamiliar landscape.

Sometime after their father's death, Blasius and Adam loaded a wagon with whatever tools, goods, and household possessions they could carry, and they left. We do not know the exact year. We do not know whether they traveled together or separately, whether they had secured positions in advance or simply set out into the unknown. We know only that they went, and that the next chapter of the family's story would unfold in the dark valleys and high ridges of the Black Forest, in a landscape quite unlike the rolling Swabian farmland of their birth.

The middle decades of the fifteenth century saw increasing mobility across the German lands. The old bonds that had tied peasants to manors were loosening. Even noble families were on the move, seeking better prospects, fleeing local conflicts, pursuing opportunities in the expanding cities or in the service of princes. The Black Forest was being colonized anew, its timber and mineral wealth attracting settlers, its monasteries — particularly the great Benedictine and Cistercian houses at St. Blasien, St. Peter, and Alpirsbach — offering the same kind of ecclesiastical economy that the Brauns had known in Ottobeuren.

The Legacy of Ottobeuren

For nearly a century, from Hans Peter Adam's birth in 1352 to Johann Bernhard's death in 1450, the Braun family called Ottobeuren home. Four generations were born there, baptized in the abbey church, married there, buried in its graveyard. They lived through the greatest catastrophe in European history and survived it. They maintained their noble status in a world where such distinctions were becoming less absolute. They adapted to the profound changes of the post-plague era while preserving their identity and their name.

When Blasius and Adam left, they carried this legacy with them. They carried the memory of the great abbey, the rhythm of the agricultural year, the obligations and privileges of their station. They carried their father's name and their grandfather's name, reaching back through Christian to Hans Peter Adam. They carried the "von" that marked them as noble, though that particle would not survive many more generations.

They also carried, whether they knew it or not, the resilience of a family that had endured. The Black Death had killed half of Europe, but the Brauns had lived. The famines and climate shifts of the fourteenth century had destroyed thousands of families, but the Brauns had persisted. They had bent with the changing times but not broken. This capacity to adapt, to survive, to move when necessary and to plant roots when possible, would prove to be their most valuable inheritance.

The fortress of Ottobeuren, that great monastery that had sheltered and shaped them, would continue its own troubled journey through history. It would reach its nadir in 1471, then slowly recover. In 1802, it would be dissolved in the secularization of church properties, and forty-eight monks would be dispersed. Yet eighteen of them would stubbornly remain, keeping the tradition alive until the abbey was re-established in 1835. Today, more than twelve and a half centuries after its founding, Benedictine monks still pray and work in Ottobeuren. The baroque basilica that now stands there, built in the eighteenth century, rises over the same ground where Hans Peter Adam Braun and his descendants once walked.

The fortress had protected them for a century. Now they would have to make their own way.
