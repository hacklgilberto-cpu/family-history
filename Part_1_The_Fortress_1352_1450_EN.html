Part One: The Fortress
Ottobeuren and the von Braun Nobility
1352–1450
In the year of our Lord 1352, when the wounds of the great pestilence were still fresh across Christendom and the Holy Roman Empire struggled to comprehend the catastrophe that had claimed perhaps half its subjects, a child was born in the Benedictine town of Ottobeuren in the Swabian lands. His name was Hans Peter Adam, and he bore the surname Braun with the particle "von" before it, marking him as a man of noble birth in an age when such distinctions meant the difference between holding land and working it, between commanding labor and performing it, between eating meat on feast days and subsisting on rye bread and thin beer. This is where our story begins, not with the grand migrations that would come later, not with the Black Forest settlements or the Brazilian wilderness, but here in this monastery town where church bells marked the hours and the great abbey dominated both the spiritual and temporal lives of every soul within its reach.

The Benedictine World

To understand Hans Peter Adam Braun and his family, one must first understand Ottobeuren itself. The abbey had stood in this valley of the Unterallgäu for nearly six hundred years by the time Hans Peter was born, founded in the year 764 by an Alemannic nobleman named Silach in the reign of the Frankish kings. It was old when Charlemagne was crowned emperor. It had weathered the Magyar raids, the investiture controversy, the rise and fall of dynasties. In 972, the Emperor Otto the Great himself had granted the abbey its great charter of freedom, the Grosser Freiheitsbrief, which freed it from all imperial taxes and services and guaranteed the monks the right to elect their own abbot without interference from king or duke or bishop.

This imperial immediacy, this direct subjection to the emperor alone with no lord standing between, was no small thing. It meant that Ottobeuren was not merely a monastery but a principality, a small ecclesiastical state within the Holy Roman Empire. The abbot was not simply a spiritual father to his monks but a temporal ruler over villages and farms, mills and forests, the administrator of justice and the collector of rents and tithes. By the fourteenth century, the abbey's territories encompassed twenty-seven villages scattered across more than two hundred and fifty square kilometers of rolling Swabian countryside, and some ten thousand souls answered to the abbot's authority in matters both sacred and profane.

The town of Ottobeuren itself clung to the abbey's walls like a child to its mother's skirts. The great church, rebuilt in Romanesque grandeur in the twelfth century, rose above the thatched roofs and narrow lanes. Its bells called the faithful to prayer at dawn, at midday, at vespers, at compline. The monks chanted the Divine Office in Latin according to the Rule of Saint Benedict, that ancient prescription for ordered communal life written in the sixth century: Ora et labora, pray and work. Seven times daily the monks gathered in their choir stalls to sing the psalms. Between these sacred hours, they copied manuscripts in the scriptorium, tended the sick in the infirmary, managed the abbey's vast agricultural operations, administered justice in the abbot's court, collected revenues, supervised construction, and trained the sons of nobles in letters and law.

The Benedictine monastery at Ottobeuren was part of a vast network of such institutions that formed the intellectual and administrative backbone of medieval Europe. In the twelfth century, under the blessed Abbot Rupert I, Ottobeuren became a center of the Hirsau reform movement, which sought to restore strict observance of the Benedictine Rule and to free monasteries from excessive secular interference. The abbey's scriptorium produced illuminated manuscripts that survive to this day in German libraries, evidence of a flourishing book culture. By the fourteenth century, however, monastic discipline had begun to slip. The abbey was wealthy, perhaps too wealthy, and it was run increasingly as an economic enterprise rather than a house of prayer. In 1356, it came under the jurisdiction of the Bishop of Augsburg, losing some of its cherished independence. By 1471, spiritual decay would reach such a nadir that only two monks remained in residence. But in 1352, when Hans Peter Adam was born, such decline was still a century away.

The World Before the Plague

Hans Peter Adam's birth came only two years after the great dying, the pestilence that contemporary chroniclers called simply die grosse Sterblichkeit, the great mortality. To understand what the Braun family had survived, one must understand what Europe had been like before the plague arrived, and what it became after.

The fourteenth century had opened with hope and then collapsed into catastrophe. The thirteenth century had been a time of relative prosperity, of expanding agriculture, of new lands brought under the plow, of forests cleared and marshes drained, of population growth that pushed settlement to the margins of cultivable land. But by 1300, that expansion had reached its limits. The climate was changing. The warm, stable weather of the High Middle Ages was giving way to what scholars now call the Little Ice Age. Winters grew longer and colder. Growing seasons shortened. Harvests failed with increasing frequency.

Then came the famines. In 1315 and 1316, rains fell without ceasing across northern Europe. The crops rotted in the fields. Grain stores dwindled. People began to starve. Chronicles tell of peasants eating cats and dogs, of parents abandoning children they could not feed, of the desperate boiling grass and tree bark to fill their bellies. Perhaps one in ten died in those terrible years, weakened by hunger and vulnerable to every passing sickness.

And then, a generation later, came something far worse.

The Black Death

The pestilence entered Europe in 1347 through the port of Caffa on the Black Sea, where Genoese merchants had maintained a trading colony. The city was under siege by Mongol forces when plague broke out in the besieging army. The Mongols, in an early act of biological warfare, catapulted infected corpses over the walls. When the Genoese fled by ship back to Italy, they brought the plague with them.

By 1348, it had spread across Italy and southern France. By 1349, it reached Germany. The chronicles tell us it arrived in Augsburg and Ulm in the summer and autumn of that year, spreading east from Strasbourg toward the great imperial cities of Swabia. Ottobeuren, lying between Augsburg and Lake Constance along the old Roman roads, cannot have escaped.

What was it like when the plague came to a place like Ottobeuren? We can only piece together the horror from scattered sources. The disease manifested in several forms, all of them terrible. The bubonic variety produced grotesque swellings in the groin and armpits, the lymph nodes swelling to the size of eggs or apples and turning black with internal hemorrhage. Hence the name that would later be given to the pestilence: the Black Death. Victims burned with fever, suffered excruciating pain, and often died within three to five days of the first symptoms appearing. The pneumonic form attacked the lungs, causing victims to cough up blood. It spread through the air from person to person and killed even more quickly, sometimes within twenty-four hours. The septicemic form caused such rapid blood poisoning that victims might go to bed healthy and be dead by morning, their bodies covered in dark purple blotches.

No one understood what caused it. Learned physicians consulted their ancient texts and blamed a conjunction of planets, a corruption of the air, an imbalance of the four humors. Some said it was God's punishment for human sin. Others blamed the Jews, accusing them of poisoning wells, and terrible pogroms swept through the Rhineland and Swabia in 1349 and 1350. In Strasbourg, two thousand Jews were burned alive. The disease paid no attention to such scapegoating. It killed rich and poor, noble and peasant, Christian and Jew, the pious and the wicked alike.

Modern scholarship estimates that the Black Death killed between 30 and 60 percent of Europe's population between 1347 and 1353. In some regions, the mortality was even higher. In Florence, tax records suggest 80 percent died. Across the German lands, tens of thousands of villages were abandoned entirely. In England, more than 1,300 villages disappeared between 1350 and 1500. The total population of Europe fell from perhaps 75 million to as few as 50 million. It would take more than a century and a half for population levels to recover. The social and economic consequences were profound and permanent. The old feudal order, already strained by the famines and climate change of the early century, could not survive the catastrophic labor shortage that followed the plague. Serfdom began its long decline. Wages rose sharply despite laws attempting to freeze them at pre-plague levels. Workers gained mobility and bargaining power. The rigid hierarchies of medieval society loosened, never to be fully restored.

A Family That Survived

And yet the Braun family survived. Hans Peter Adam Braun was born on the seventh day of November in 1352, in the town of Reimlingen in the Ries basin north of Ottobeuren. His father's name is lost to us, but we know his mother was Maria Catharina Stiff, born in 1357 in Ottobeuren itself. The Stiff family was local, rooted in the abbey's lands, part of that class of free peasants and minor gentry who formed the backbone of rural society.

The couple married on the first of July, 1380, in Reimlingen. Hans Peter was twenty-seven years old, Maria Catharina twenty-three. These were typical ages for marriage in a post-plague world where land had become available and young people could afford to establish households earlier than their parents' generation had managed. The wedding would have taken place at the church door, as was customary, with the priest blessing their union and the couple exchanging vows in the vernacular German before witnesses from both families. Afterward, there would have been a feast, modest or lavish depending on the family's means, with beer and bread and perhaps some meat, and dancing that the local priest might or might not have approved of.

They made their home in Ottobeuren. Hans Peter bore the designation "von Braun," which marked him as noble, but what did nobility mean in such a time and place? It did not mean vast estates or coats of arms emblazoned on castle walls. It meant, perhaps, exemption from certain taxes and labor services. It meant the right to bear arms and to own a war horse. It meant precedence in church and at table, the right to sit nearer the lord at feast, to be addressed with certain courtesies. It meant, most practically, the holding of land not as a tenant but as an inheritor, with the right to pass that land to one's children.

In 1380, at the time of Hans Peter's marriage, their first child was already born or soon would be. Christian Braun came into the world that year, the first of what would be a single surviving line. In a time of high infant mortality, when perhaps half of all children died before reaching adulthood, the survival of any child to maturity was a victory.

Life in a Monastery Town

What was daily life like for the Braun family in Ottobeuren at the end of the fourteenth century? We can reconstruct it from manorial records, from chronicles, from archaeological evidence, from the dry administrative documents that have somehow survived six centuries of wars and fires and floods.

The family would have lived in a house of timber frame with walls of wattle and daub, the gaps between the wooden beams filled with woven sticks and covered in mud mixed with straw and dung. The roof would have been thatched with rye straw, which needed replacing every decade or so. Inside, the house might have had two or three rooms: a main hall where the family cooked and ate and worked during winter months, a chamber where they slept, perhaps a separate room for storage. The floors would have been packed earth, covered with rushes that were swept out and replaced when they grew too fouled with mud and food scraps.

Heat came from a central hearth in the main room. In a noble household, there might have been a proper fireplace built into the wall, with a chimney to carry the smoke out, but such amenities were still rare outside the great houses. More likely, the smoke simply rose to the roof beams and seeped out through the thatch. The family rose with the sun and went to bed soon after dark. Candles were expensive, made of tallow or beeswax. Ordinary people relied on firelight and perhaps a rush dipped in fat, which gave a dim, smoky illumination.

As a family of minor nobility, the Brauns would have had obligations to the abbot and rights over some parcel of land, perhaps a few dozen acres of fields and meadow and woodland. They would have owed rents in coin or kind, labor services at harvest time, military service if the abbot needed armed men. In exchange, they had security of tenure and the right to pass their holding to their children.

The agricultural year governed everything. In spring, the fields needed plowing and sowing. The great wooden plow, pulled by oxen, cut furrows in the heavy clay soil. Rye and oats were the main crops in this part of Swabia, better suited to the cool, damp climate than wheat or barley. Women and children followed behind the plow, breaking up clods and scattering seed. Through summer, the fields needed weeding, the meadows mowing for hay to feed the livestock through winter. In August and September came the harvest, that desperate race against weather and time when every able body was needed in the fields. The grain had to be cut with sickles, tied into sheaves, carried to the barns, threshed, and winnowed before the autumn rains came.

The post-plague economy was characterized by chronic labor shortages and rising wages. Landlords struggled to find workers for their fields. Across Europe, governments tried to freeze wages at pre-plague levels and to restrict workers' mobility, but these laws were widely ignored. Workers could demand higher pay, better conditions, and more freedom. The old manorial system, in which peasants were bound to a lord's land and owed him labor services, was breaking down. By 1400, most agricultural labor in southern Germany was performed by free workers hired for wages or by tenant farmers who paid rent in coin rather than labor. This shift gave peasants more autonomy but also more insecurity. A bad harvest could mean starvation for families who no longer had a lord's granary to fall back on. The chroniclers of the time noted with disapproval that peasants were wearing better clothes, eating meat more often, and showing less deference to their betters. The old order was dying, though few yet recognized it.

The Next Generation

Christian Braun, born in 1380, grew to manhood in this changing world. Around 1399, at the age of nineteen, he married Eva Catherina, a woman whose surname has not come down to us but who was born in 1382 in Ottobeuren. Like his father, Christian married a local woman, keeping the family rooted in the abbey lands.

Their son Johann Bernhard Braun was born in the year 1400, at the very turning of the century. His birth came as the old century ended and the new began, though such calendrical milestones meant little to most people, who reckoned time by feast days and harvests, by the reigns of emperors and popes, by the cycles of birth and death in their own families.

Johann Bernhard's childhood in early fifteenth-century Ottobeuren would have been shaped by the rhythms of the church year. Christmas and Easter, Pentecost and the feast of All Saints, the Virgin Mary's assumption and the celebration of local patron saints; these marked the passage of time more surely than any calendar. On Sundays and feast days, work ceased and the family attended Mass at the abbey church. They would have stood in the nave with the other laypeople, the great rood screen separating them from the choir where the monks chanted their Latin offices. The priest elevated the Host and the bell rang and everyone knelt. Most could not understand the Latin words, but they knew the gestures, the standing and kneeling and crossing, the ritual that connected them to God and to their ancestors who had performed these same motions for generations.

Johann Bernhard would have been baptized within days of his birth, the ceremony performed at the abbey church with a priest pouring holy water over his head while godparents held him and spoke vows on his behalf. If he survived the dangerous first year of life, when so many infants died, he would have been raised partly by his mother and partly by the community of children who played in the lanes and alleys of Ottobeuren. By age seven or so, he would have begun learning his father's work, following Christian into the fields, watching how to plow and sow and reap, how to manage tenants and collect rents, how to present oneself before the abbot's court, how to behave as a man of his station.

Johann Bernhard's Marriage

Around 1425, at the age of twenty-five, Johann Bernhard Braun married Eva Catherina Bauer. She was nineteen years old, born in 1406 in Ottobeuren to Christian Bauer and his wife. The Bauer name meant "farmer," though it was misleading as a marker of status. Many families bore occupational surnames that no longer reflected their actual work. Some Bauers were indeed farmers, but others had moved into trade or minor administrative posts. Christian Bauer, Eva's father, had lived from 1382 to 1434, and his daughter's marriage to a Braun, a family with the "von" distinction, suggests the Bauers had some standing in the community.

The marriage was almost certainly arranged by the fathers, as was customary. Young people of this class did not marry for romantic love, though affection might grow over time. They married to consolidate land, to forge alliances between families, to produce heirs who would carry on the family name and inherit its property. The wedding contract would have specified what each party brought to the marriage: land, livestock, household goods, coin. These arrangements were serious business, often negotiated over months and recorded in detail to prevent future disputes.

Johann Bernhard and Eva Catherina established their household in Ottobeuren, continuing the family's three-generation residence in the shadow of the abbey. Their first child, Jacob Braun, was born in 1420, possibly before the formal marriage, which was not uncommon when a bride was already pregnant. Five years into the marriage came a momentous year: 1430 brought not one but two sons, or perhaps the records conflate two births that occurred within a year of each other.

Adam Luppert von Braun and Blasius von Braun entered the world in 1430. The presence of the "von" in their names is significant. The family was maintaining its noble status into the fourth generation, passing it down from Hans Peter Adam through Christian to Johann Bernhard and now to his sons. Blasius was named for Saint Blaise, the Armenian bishop martyred in 316, whose feast day fell on February third. Blaise was particularly venerated in the German lands as a patron saint of throat ailments, and on his feast day the priest would bless throats with two crossed candles. The name suggests that the family maintained its Catholic devotions in a time when faith was still universal and unquestioned, decades before Martin Luther would nail his theses to a church door and split Christendom in two.

The World of 1430

The year of Blasius and Adam's birth came eighty years after the first plague outbreak. Europe had begun to recover, though slowly. Population was creeping back up. Villages that had been abandoned were being resettled. Fields that had gone to scrub were being plowed again. But the plague had not disappeared. It returned in waves throughout the fifteenth century, striking here and there with seemingly random cruelty, carrying off the young and healthy, sparing others for no reason anyone could discern.

In Ottobeuren specifically, the abbey was entering a period of decline that would reach its nadir in 1471, when only two monks remained. The great landholding monasteries of the Middle Ages were struggling to maintain themselves in an age of changing economics and shifting power relations. The Black Death had disrupted their labor force and their revenue streams. Younger sons of noble families, who in earlier centuries might have been sent to monasteries to become monks and administrators, were now more likely to seek their fortunes in the expanding cities or in the military service of princes and emperors. The abbey at Ottobeuren, once a beacon of learning and piety, was increasingly run as a business enterprise, its abbots more concerned with managing estates and collecting revenues than with the spiritual life of their monks.

Yet for a family like the Brauns, the abbey remained the center of their world. The church bells still ordered the day. The abbot still held jurisdiction over local disputes. The abbey's court still administered justice. The monastery still owned the mills where grain was ground and the ovens where bread was baked, and every household paid its dues for these services.

The End of an Era

Johann Bernhard's wife, Eva Catherina Bauer, died in 1448 at the age of forty-two. She was buried in the abbey's graveyard, that consecrated ground that had received the bodies of Ottobeuren's dead for seven centuries. No stone marked her grave, or if it did, it has not survived. Such monuments were reserved for abbots and great nobles, not for the wives of minor gentry. But her sons remembered her, and her name has come down to us through the careful records that someone, somewhere, maintained across the centuries.

Two years later, in 1450, Johann Bernhard Braun himself died. He was fifty years old, which was not a bad age for the time. He had survived infancy, the most dangerous time of life. He had survived to adulthood and married and fathered children. He had lived through plague years and harvest failures, through cold winters and wet summers. He had maintained his family's status and passed it on to his sons. In the great ledger of medieval lives, this counted as success.

He left behind three sons: Jacob, now thirty years old; Adam Luppert, twenty; and Blasius, also twenty. The younger two would live long lives, Blasius until 1510 and Adam until 1499, spanning the entire latter half of the fifteenth century. They would witness the end of the Middle Ages and the beginning of the early modern period. They would see the Ottoman Turks conquer Constantinople in 1453, ending the thousand-year Byzantine Empire. They would live through the Wars of the Roses in England, the consolidation of France under Louis XI, the marriage of Ferdinand and Isabella that would unite Spain. Adam would die just seven years after Christopher Columbus reached the Americas, though he might never have heard of that voyage.

But more immediately relevant to our story, Johann Bernhard's sons would be the generation that left Ottobeuren. The family that had been rooted there for at least four generations, that had survived the Black Death and its aftermath, that had maintained its noble status through changing times, would not remain in the abbey's shadow forever. Somewhere in the records, in the gaps between what we know and what we can only infer, the decision was made to leave.

The middle decades of the fifteenth century saw increasing mobility across the German lands. The plague had created opportunities as well as tragedies. Empty lands could be claimed, vacant positions filled. The old bonds that had tied peasants to manors were loosening. Even noble families were on the move, seeking better prospects, fleeing local conflicts, pursuing opportunities in the expanding cities or in the service of princes. The Black Forest, that great expanse of mountains and valleys to the west of Swabia, was being opened to new settlement. Monasteries there needed administrators, villages needed craftsmen, lords needed soldiers and stewards. The restless younger sons of minor nobility could find roles there that Ottobeuren, with its established hierarchies and limited opportunities, could not offer.

The Legacy of Ottobeuren

For nearly a century, from Hans Peter Adam's birth in 1352 to Johann Bernhard's death in 1450, the Braun family called Ottobeuren home. Four generations were born there, baptized in the abbey church, married there, buried in its graveyard. They lived through the greatest catastrophe in European history and survived it. They maintained their status as nobility in a world where such distinctions were becoming less absolute. They adapted to the profound economic and social changes of the post-plague era while preserving their identity and their name.

When Blasius and Adam left Ottobeuren sometime after their father's death, they carried with them this legacy. They carried the memory of the great abbey and its bells, the rhythm of the agricultural year, the obligations and privileges of their station. They carried their father's name and their grandfather's name, reaching back through Christian to Hans Peter Adam. They carried the "von" that marked them as noble, though that particle would not survive many more generations.

They also carried, whether they knew it or not, the resilience of a family that had endured. The Black Death had killed half of Europe, but the Brauns had lived. The famines and climate change of the fourteenth century had destroyed thousands of families, but the Brauns had persisted. They had bent with the changing times but not broken. This capacity to adapt, to survive, to move when necessary and to plant roots when possible, would prove to be their most valuable inheritance.

The fortress of Ottobeuren, that great monastery that had sheltered and shaped them, would continue its own troubled journey through history. It would reach its nadir in 1471, then slowly recover. In 1802, it would be dissolved in the secularization of church properties, and forty-eight monks would be dispersed. Yet eighteen of them would stubbornly remain, keeping the tradition alive until the abbey was re-established in 1835. Today, more than twelve and a half centuries after its founding, Benedictine monks still pray and work in Ottobeuren. The baroque basilica that now stands there, built in the eighteenth century, rises over the same ground where Hans Peter Adam Braun and his descendants once walked.

But the Brauns themselves were gone from Ottobeuren by 1450, headed west into the Black Forest, into new lands and new lives, carrying with them only their names and their memories and whatever tools and goods they could load onto a wagon. The next part of their story would unfold in different valleys, under different lords, in a landscape of deep forests and high mountains quite unlike the rolling Swabian farmland of their birth.

The fortress had protected them for a century. Now they would have to make their own way.
