Part Five: Terra Nova
The Carpenter's House and the Meeting of Two Lines
1919–1955
Two families, two countries, two journeys across the Atlantic. The Hackls from the volcanic hills of Styria, arriving at Santos on a Dutch steamship in February 1925, bound for the coffee frontier of northern Paraná. The Würzburgers from the vine-covered slopes of the Glottertal, arriving by a route that has been lost to the records, bound for a German colony on the cool highlands south of Curitiba. They did not know each other. They came from opposite ends of the German-speaking world, separated by a thousand kilometers of Alpine geography and five centuries of separate family history. But Brazil, that vast and improbable country, would bring them together. It would take thirty years and a generation born on Brazilian soil, but the fortress and the axe would find each other at last.

A Marriage in Ohrensbach

On the seventh of October, 1919, in the hamlet of Ohrensbach in the Glottertal, Josef Würzburger married Anna Luise Gehri. He was twenty-six, a Schreiner, a carpenter and joiner, the son of Kasimir Würzburger and Rosa Beck of Föhrental. She was thirty, born in Wildtal near Gundelfingen, the daughter of Georg Gehri and Afra Schwehr, whose story we have already told. Through Afra, Anna Luise carried in her blood the inheritance of the Schwehr line, which reached back through Heuweiler to Sankt Peter and, beyond that, through the mists of poorly documented centuries, to the von Braun nobility of Ottobeuren.

It was a marriage of two Glottertal families. The Würzburgers had been in Föhrental since the 1730s; the Gehri family was established in Wildtal, just a few kilometers away. The wedding took place in the valley where both families had roots, in the parish that had recorded their births and deaths for generations. It must have seemed, in that autumn of 1919, like a continuation of everything that had come before: another marriage in the same church, another entry in the same register, another generation taking its place in the ancient cycle of the valley.

The church in Glottertal — Where Josef Würzburger and Anna Luise Gehri married in 1919, and where Anna Luise was buried in 1925

But the world outside the Glottertal was in ruins. Germany had lost the war. The Kaiser had abdicated. The economy was shattered. The Weimar Republic, fragile and unstable, was struggling to feed its people. Inflation was beginning its catastrophic rise. And in the Glottertal, the old problems persisted: too many people, too little land, too few ways to make a living. Josef Würzburger was a skilled craftsman, a Schreiner trained in the Black Forest tradition of precision woodworking, but skill alone did not guarantee survival in a world where the currency in your pocket lost half its value between breakfast and supper.

Anna Luise

Anna Luise Gehri was born on the fifteenth of May, 1889, in Wildtal. She grew up in the shadow of the Black Forest, in a landscape of vineyards and small farms, in a family that had been touched by emigration on every side. Her Ruth uncles had gone to Canada. Her Schwehr uncle Georg had followed them to Waterloo, Ontario. The pull of the New World was a constant presence in the families of the Glottertal, a gravitational force that tugged at every generation.

Anna Luise and Josef had their first years together in the Glottertal. Their daughter Maria Theresia Würzburger was born on the eighteenth of July, 1920, in Ohrensbach. Their son August Würzburger followed on the twenty-ninth of December, 1921, but lived only weeks, dying on the ninth of February, 1922. Then came Elisabeth Würzburger, born on the twenty-fourth of November, 1922, in Ohrensbach. Elisabeth. The girl who would one day marry Friedrich Hackl and become the grandmother of the person for whom this history is being written.

And then, on the thirtieth of June, 1925, Anna Luise died. She was thirty-six years old. The cause is not recorded in the genealogical data, but the bare fact is devastating enough. Elisabeth was two years and seven months old. Maria Theresia was nearly five. Josef Würzburger was thirty-two, a widower with two small daughters in a country that offered him nothing.

Anna Luise was buried in Ohrensbach, in the Glottertal, in the same soil where her Würzburger and Gehri ancestors lay. She had lived her entire life within a radius of perhaps ten kilometers, from Wildtal where she was born to Ohrensbach where she died. She never saw Brazil. She never saw her daughter Elisabeth grow up, marry, or have children of her own. She exists in the family record as a name and a pair of dates: 1889–1925. But without her, without this woman who died young in a Black Forest hamlet, nothing that follows in this story would have happened. Elisabeth was her daughter. And Elisabeth's son was Frederico. And Frederico's son was Gilberto. The line runs through Anna Luise like a river through a narrow gorge: briefly visible, quickly gone, but carrying everything forward.

The Second Marriage

Josef did not remain a widower long. On the twelfth of June, 1926, less than a year after Anna Luise's death, he married Maria Bühler. She was twenty-six, born on the twenty-third of January, 1900, in Ottenhöfen, the daughter of Andreas Bühler and Barbara Lenz. The speed of the remarriage was not callousness but necessity. A man with two small children needed a wife. A household needed a woman to run it. The Glottertal in 1926, like the Heuweiler of a century earlier, was a place where marriage was as much a practical arrangement as an emotional bond.

Josef and Maria would have two children together: Anna Rosa Würzburger and Erich Würzburger. These half-siblings of Elisabeth's would later remain in Castro, Paraná, where they built their lives in the colony their father had helped to found. The family dynamics that this second marriage created, the division between the children of the first wife and the children of the second, would echo through the generations in ways both predictable and painful. Elisabeth, the daughter of the first marriage, would eventually find herself at a distance from the Würzburger family in Castro, a distance measured not in kilometers but in the quiet estrangements that second marriages and blended families so often produce.

Maria Theresia, Josef and Anna Luise's eldest daughter, did not survive childhood. Born in 1920, she died on the twenty-fourth of June, 1932, in Ohrensbach, at the age of eleven. By the time Josef departed for Brazil in 1933, he had lost his first wife, his first son August, and his first daughter Maria Theresia. Of the three children he had with Anna Luise, only Elisabeth survived. She was ten years old when her half-sister died, and eleven when her father took the family across the Atlantic. What it meant to be that child, the sole survivor of her mother's line, carried to a new continent by a father who had remarried and started a second family, we can only imagine.

Terra Nova

In 1933, Josef Würzburger made his decision. He would leave Germany. The parish Familienblätter, those meticulous family sheets maintained by the Glottertal church, record it in a single line: Wanderte 1933 nach Brasilien, Staat Santa Catharina, Siedlung Terra Nova bei Castro. "Emigrated 1933 to Brazil, state of Santa Catarina, settlement Terra Nova near Castro." The state was recorded incorrectly on the documents, a clerical error that would persist in the family records. The settlement was not in Santa Catarina but in Paraná, in the municipality of Castro, one of the oldest towns in the state, founded in 1778 on the highland plateau known as the Campos Gerais.

The timing was not accidental. On the eighteenth of July, 1933, the Fazenda Marilândia, a property of 1,300 alqueires about ten kilometers from the town of Castro, was purchased by the Gesellschaft für Siedlung im Auslande, the Society for Colonization Abroad, based in Berlin. The purchase was facilitated by the German consul in Curitiba, who had been working to establish a German agricultural colony in the Paraná highlands. The society invited German families who were struggling financially to start new lives on the land. They called it Colônia Terra Nova: New Land.

One hundred and sixteen families received plots in the colony. Each family was given twelve alqueires, roughly twenty-nine hectares, of land divided into areas for cultivation, pasture, and settlement. They received a provisional house, seeds, and basic tools. The conditions were clear: you had to farm the land for at least two years. You had to build your own permanent house. You had to make it work.

For Josef Würzburger, a trained Schreiner who had spent his life working wood, the requirement to farm was a challenge. He was a carpenter, not a farmer. But the colony needed carpenters perhaps more than it needed farmers, because before anyone could farm, someone had to build. Houses, barns, fences, tools, furniture: everything in the colony had to be constructed from scratch, from the timber of the surrounding forests. A man who knew how to shape wood with precision, who had learned his craft in the Black Forest tradition of joinery, was worth his weight in seed corn.

How Josef and his family reached Brazil remains uncertain. Unlike the Hackl family, whose arrival on the SS Orania at Santos in February 1925 is documented in the Brazilian National Archives, the Würzburger journey has left no trace in the immigrant records at the Museu da Imigração in São Paulo. This absence suggests that they may not have entered through Santos at all. They may have arrived at Paranaguá, the port city on the Paraná coast, which was the natural point of entry for immigrants destined for the Paraná highlands. From Paranaguá, a railroad climbed the steep Serra do Mar escarpment to Curitiba, and from Curitiba it was a day's journey overland to Castro. This route would have bypassed São Paulo entirely, which would explain why the Würzburger name does not appear in the Hospedaria dos Imigrantes records. But this is speculation. The documents, if they exist, are in the hands of the family in Castro, and the family in Castro does not share.

Castro, Paraná — One of the oldest cities in the state, near which Josef Würzburger built his home in the Colônia Terra Nova

The House He Built

Josef Würzburger, who now answered to the Portuguese spelling José, built his house in Terra Nova with his own hands. He used the timber of the region, the native hardwoods of the Paraná highlands, Araucaria pine and imbuia and cedar, and he shaped them with the techniques he had brought from Germany, the mortise-and-tenon joints, the careful fitting of beam to beam, the precision that a Schreiner learned through years of apprenticeship in the Black Forest workshops. The house was not large, but it was solid. It was built to last. And it has lasted. It stands today, nearly a century after it was raised, still inhabited, still sheltering the family that José founded.

There is something deeply fitting about this. The Würzburger line, traced back through Kasimir and Barbara and Joseph and Georg and Simon, had been rooted in the Glottertal for two hundred years. They had lived in Black Forest farmhouses, those massive timber structures with their steep roofs and thick walls. And when José arrived in Brazil, in a colony where everything had to be made from nothing, the first thing he did was build a house in the way his people had always built houses: with wood, with skill, with the patient craft of a man who understood that a properly joined beam would outlast the man who shaped it.

The climate helped. Castro sits at about a thousand meters above sea level on the Campos Gerais plateau, a landscape of rolling grasslands and Araucaria forests that bears a striking resemblance to parts of central Europe. The temperatures are mild, averaging about seventeen degrees Celsius, with cool winters that can bring frost. It rains less than in the tropical lowlands. The air is dry and clear. For German immigrants who had endured the brutal heat of the São Paulo coffee frontier, or who had heard stories of malaria and yellow fever in the northern settlements, the Paraná highlands must have felt like a homecoming. Not Germany, exactly, but close enough. Close enough to breathe easily. Close enough to sleep without mosquito nets. Close enough to recognize the seasons.

The colony attracted both Catholics and Lutherans, Germans from diverse regions with different dialects and customs, all united by the common experience of dislocation and the common need to start over. They planted wheat, rye, and potatoes, the crops they knew from home. They kept dairy cattle. They joined cooperatives, notably the Cooperativa Castrolanda and the Cooperativa Batavo, Dutch-founded agricultural cooperatives that served the broader immigrant community of the Campos Gerais. Over the decades, the families of Terra Nova built themselves into the regional economy, moving from subsistence farming to commercial agriculture and livestock. Today, Castro is the largest milk-producing municipality in Brazil, and the descendants of the German colonists of Terra Nova are part of the agribusiness establishment that drives the regional economy.

Colônia Terra Nova today is a rural neighborhood about ten kilometers from downtown Castro, still home to some sixty families descended from the original 116 who arrived in 1933. Historic German-style houses with timber framing still stand along the colony's roads. The community maintains German traditions with remarkable tenacity: festivals featuring eisbein and braten mit soße, music and dancing, and a café colonial breakfast of broa, waffles, cakes, jams, and fresh dairy that would not be out of place in a Schwarzwald Gasthof. Some residents still speak German among themselves. On the colony's anniversary, there are parades of tractors and horse-drawn carts through the settlement. It is, in its quiet way, a piece of the Glottertal transplanted to the Brazilian highlands, a living monument to the stubbornness of memory and the persistence of craft.

Elisabeth

Elisabeth Würzburger arrived in Brazil as a girl of ten or eleven, carried across the Atlantic by a father she shared with a stepmother and half-siblings. She had lost her mother at three, her sister Maria Theresia at ten, and now her country at eleven. Everything she had known, the Glottertal, the church at Ohrensbach, the grave where her mother lay, was gone, replaced by a colony in the Brazilian highlands where the language was strange and the future was uncertain.

She grew up in Terra Nova, in the house her father built. She learned Portuguese, the language her mother never spoke. She became Brazilian, as immigrant children do, absorbing the new country through its sounds and smells and tastes while the old country receded into the stories told at the dinner table in a German that grew more halting with each passing year.

And at some point, through circumstances the records do not preserve, she met a young man named Friedrich Hackl. He was called Fritz by the old folks and Frederico by the Brazilians. He had arrived on the Orania in 1925, an eleven-year-old Austrian boy, the son of a porter from Graz. He had grown up on the coffee frontier of northern Paraná, in the red earth and tropical heat that was everything the Campos Gerais was not. How a Hackl from Mandaguaçu met a Würzburger from Castro, across the vast distances of the Paraná interior, is a question the family cannot answer. Perhaps it was through the German-speaking community that connected the scattered immigrant settlements. Perhaps it was through the Catholic Church, which served as a social network for the faithful across the state. Perhaps it was simple chance, the kind of improbable encounter that happens a thousand times a day in a country the size of a continent.

On the third of December, 1955, in Mandaguaçu, Paraná, Friedrich Hackl married Elisabeth Würzburger. He was forty-one. She was thirty-three. Both had been born in the German-speaking lands of Europe and raised in the immigrant communities of Brazil. Both carried surnames that encoded the history of the families behind them: Hackl, the small axe, the tool of the working man; Würzburger, the name of a distant Franconian city that had somehow attached itself to a family in the Black Forest and followed them across the ocean.

The Fortress and the Axe

With this marriage, two stories that had been running in parallel for more than five hundred years finally converged. The von Braun line from Ottobeuren, which had become the Schwehrs of Sankt Peter, which had become the Schwehrs of Heuweiler, which had merged with the Gehrls of Wildtal and produced Afra, who married Georg Gehri and bore Anna Luise, who married Josef Würzburger and bore Elisabeth: this line, stretching back to 1352 and a child born in the shadow of a Benedictine abbey, met at last the Hackl line from Sankt Anna am Aigen, which had moved from the volcanic hills to the streets of Graz, from Graz to the port of Amsterdam, from Amsterdam to the coffee plantations of São Paulo, and from there to the pioneer settlements of Paraná.

Friedrich and Elisabeth settled eventually in São Paulo, the great city that absorbed so many immigrant families into its sprawling urban fabric. Their son Frederico Hackl was born on the fifteenth of April, 1957, in Mandaguaçu. He would grow up, marry Solange de Melo Ribeiro, and father two sons. The elder, Gilberto Hackl, was born on the twentieth of December, 1982, in São Paulo. Gilberto would one day sit down at a computer and begin tracing these lines backward through the centuries, through the parish registers and baptismal certificates and ship manifests and Familienblätter, assembling the fragments of a story that no single ancestor had ever known in its entirety.

Friedrich and Elisabeth both died in São Paulo in the 1990s, far from the lands where they were born. Josef Würzburger, the carpenter who built his house in Terra Nova, died in Castro. The exact date is held in documents that remain in the possession of the family in Castro, alongside the records of his second marriage and his later years in the colony he helped to build.

Of Josef's children from his second marriage, Anna Rosa Würzburger remained in Castro and raised her family there. Her brother Erich Würzburger, according to family memory, returned to Germany and became a Catholic priest, a detail that, if true, would complete a remarkable arc: a Würzburger line that began in the shadow of the Black Forest monasteries, that left Germany for Brazil, and that sent one son back across the ocean to serve the same Church that had baptized his ancestors in the Glottertal for two hundred years. Old photographs, shown by Elisabeth to her grandchildren, preserve the image of a man in priestly vestments whom she called a kind of uncle. But like so much in this story, the details have been softened by time, and certainty remains elusive.

But the house stands. The house José built with Araucaria pine and Black Forest joinery, in a colony called Terra Nova, in a country called Brazil, on a continent that Hans Peter Adam von Braun of Ottobeuren could not have conceived of and that Johan Hackl of Sankt Anna am Aigen never dreamed he would see. The house stands, and the family continues, and the story goes on.

Epilogue: The Circle

There is a detail about how Friedrich and Elisabeth found each other that adds one more layer of improbability to this story. According to family accounts, they did not meet by chance. They were matched through a pairing system, a kind of matrimonial correspondence service, perhaps through newspaper announcements or community networks that connected German-speaking immigrants across the vast distances of the Brazilian interior. A Hackl from the tropical north of Paraná and a Würzburger from the temperate highlands in the south, brought together not by proximity or accident but by the deliberate search for a partner who shared a language, a faith, and a cultural world. The fortress did not stumble upon the axe. Someone, somewhere, made an introduction.

And there is a final symmetry that no one planned. In the 1850s, the Ruth brothers of Heuweiler emigrated to the Waterloo region of Ontario, Canada. They settled in Woolwich and Elmira and the surrounding countryside, establishing a German-speaking community that would persist for generations. A century and a half later, Gilberto Hackl, great-great-great-grandson of the Schwehr and Ruth families of Heuweiler, descendant of the Würzburgers of Föhrental, grandson of Friedrich Hackl and Elisabeth Würzburger, moved to the Kitchener-Waterloo region of Ontario. He lived there. His son Michael was born there, in 2020, in the same landscape where his distant Ruth cousins had put down roots in the 1850s.

Kitchener, Ontario — The city once called Berlin, where the Ruth brothers settled in the 1850s, and where Gilberto Hackl's son Michael was born in 2020

The circle closes. Not perfectly, not deliberately, but with the quiet insistence of a pattern that repeats across centuries. The same patch of Canadian soil that drew the Ruth brothers from the Black Forest in the age of sailing ships drew a Hackl from Brazil in the age of jet aircraft. The names had changed. The languages had shifted. The routes were different. But the destination was the same, as if the place itself had been waiting.

This is what a family history teaches us, if it teaches us anything at all. That the past is not past. That the decisions made by people whose names we barely know, in villages we have never visited, in centuries we can only imagine, echo forward through time in ways that are impossible to predict and impossible to ignore. Hans Peter Adam von Braun, born in 1352, could not have known that his descendants would one day live in a country that would not be discovered by Europeans for another 140 years. Leonhard Hackl, carrying luggage at the Graz Hauptbahnhof, could not have known that his great-great-grandson would one day trace his baptismal record in the Diocesan Archive. Josef Würzburger, fitting a beam into place in the house he was building in Terra Nova, could not have known that someone would one day sleep in that house and feel, in the silence of the Paraná night, the presence of all the generations that had led to that moment.

The Hackl arms and the arms of Würzburg — The axe and the fortress, united at last in Brazil

But here we are. The fortress sheltered them. The axe shaped the world they built. And the story, which began with a child born during the Black Death in a Swabian monastery town, continues in São Paulo, in Kitchener, in whatever city or country or continent the next generation chooses to call home.

The valley does not hold them anymore. But it made them who they are.
