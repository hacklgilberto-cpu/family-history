The Fortress and the Axe
A Family History of the Hackl and Würzburger Lines
1352 — Present
This is the story of two families that crossed the Atlantic from different corners of the German-speaking world and found each other in Brazil. It begins in 1352 — not with the Hackl name or the Würzburger name, but with a family called von Braun, in a Benedictine monastery town in Swabia. From that distant root, through six centuries and dozens of surnames, two lines descend: one through the Black Forest to the Glottertal, the other through the volcanic hills of Styria to the streets of Graz. Both end in Paraná, Brazil, where a carpenter's daughter from the highlands married a porter's son from the tropics, and a new family began.

The Families in This Story

When Gilberto Hackl, born in São Paulo in 1982, began tracing his family's origins, he expected to find two lines: his grandfather Friedrich Hackl from Austria and his grandmother Elisabeth Würzburger from Germany. What he found instead was a web of families stretching back nearly seven hundred years, each name a thread in a tapestry that spans the breadth of Central Europe.

On the Würzburger side, the trail leads back through the Black Forest villages of the Glottertal and Heuweiler, through a succession of families whose names still echo in those valleys today: Würzburger of Föhrental (from the 1710s), Gehri of Wildtal, Schwehr of Heuweiler (from the 1680s), Gschwander of Glottertal, Schill, Merz, Reichenbach, Beck, Rüd/Ruth of Simonswald, Wehrle, Widmann, Ihringer, Strecker, Schurhammer, Blattmann, and Raufer. Further back, the Schwehr line passes through the monastery lands of Sankt Peter as Schwär, then as Rueff/Ruff, Dold, and Braun/von Braun, reaching the Benedictine abbey town of Ottobeuren in the year 1352.

On the Hackl side, the trail leads to the wine-growing hills of southeastern Styria, Austria: Hackl/Hakl of Sankt Anna am Aigen and Hochstraden (from the 1830s), Gigl, Küschal of Schöder in Upper Styria, and Eberhard of St. Peter am Kammersberg.

The title of this history, "The Fortress and the Axe," encodes the two oldest names in the story. The fortress is Ottobeuren, the great Benedictine abbey that sheltered the von Braun family in the fourteenth century. The axe is the Hackl name itself, derived from the Middle High German word for a small hatchet, the tool of the working man who cleared forests and shaped wood. When Friedrich Hackl married Elisabeth Würzburger in Brazil in 1955, these two traditions — the monastic shelter and the woodsman's labor, separated by five hundred years and a thousand kilometers — were united at last.

The deep ancestry — From Hans Peter Adam von Braun in Ottobeuren (1352) through the Dold, Rueff, Schwär, and Schwehr lines to Afra Schwehr and Georg Gehri

The convergence — The Würzburger, Schwehr, Gschwander, Beck, Schill, Merz, and Ruth lines joining through Josef Würzburger and Anna Luise Gehri to Elisabeth, who married Friedrich Hackl

The research behind this history draws on parish Familienblätter from the Glottertal, baptismal and marriage records from the Diocese of Graz-Seckau, GEDCOM genealogical data, ship passenger manifests from the Brazilian National Archives, Austrian passports, Brazilian birth and death certificates, and the records of the Colônia Terra Nova in Castro, Paraná. Some documents were read in their original handwritten German and Portuguese; others were reconstructed from fragmentary sources. Not every date is certain. Not every connection is proven beyond doubt. But the broad arc of the story — from Swabia to the Black Forest, from Styria to Graz, from Europe to Brazil, and from Brazil to wherever the next generation carries these names — is as solid as the house that Josef Würzburger built in Terra Nova with his own hands.
